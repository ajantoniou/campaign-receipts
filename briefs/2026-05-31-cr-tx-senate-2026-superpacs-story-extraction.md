# Story Extraction — cr-tx-senate-2026-superpacs — 2026-05-31 (CORRECTED, supersedes inverted version)

## 1. Central tension
Over $110 million of disguised-name super-PAC money tried to pick Texas's next senator — and in both primaries it backfired. A Texan watching a TV ad from "Texans for a Conservative Majority" had no way to know the warm, home-state name was the public face of a pro-Cornyn machine that spent $39 million to STOP Ken Paxton. Paxton won anyway. The money was louder than ever, and the voters still didn't do what it paid for.

## 2. Transformation arc
The viewer starts feeling the ads come from large crowds of people like them — "Texans," a "majority," a "Lone Star" — and that all that money must decide the outcome. They end feeling two things at once: the crowds were a handful of billionaires routing checks through pass-throughs, AND the money lost — Paxton beat the $39M spent to stop him, and Talarico, who runs against super-PAC money, won with $9M of it spent for him. The disguise is real; the ballot still beat the checkbook.

## 3. Key reveal
The backfire. "Texans for a Conservative Majority" wasn't a crowd — it was a pro-Cornyn vehicle, top-funded by beer billionaire John Nau at $7.9M, that spent $39 million AGAINST Paxton. And Paxton won the runoff 63.8%. The friendly name was a costume; the giant number was a bet that lost. Same pattern across the aisle: Lone Star Rising spent $9M against Crockett to clear the lane for Talarico — and Talarico, the candidate who campaigns against this exact kind of money, won.

## 4. Emotional stakes
If the viewer keeps scrolling, the next "grassroots Texans" ad works the way it was built to — borrowing the trust of a crowd that doesn't exist. If they watch, they walk away with the thing the friendly name was designed to take: the ability to ask "who actually paid for this?" and get a real answer on the free FEC record — and the quiet reassurance that their one ballot just out-voted $39 million.

## 5. Narrative beats (5-9)
1. A Texan sees an ad signed "Texans for a Conservative Majority" and feels, for a second, that a crowd of neighbors is behind it.
2. The price tag lands: over $110 million in outside money moved through this one Senate race — the most of any race this cycle — most of it spent before most people were paying attention.
3. The first name is pulled apart: "Texans for a Conservative Majority" is a pro-Cornyn vehicle, top-funded by beer-distribution billionaire John Nau at $7.9 million, and it spent $39 million against one Republican — Ken Paxton.
