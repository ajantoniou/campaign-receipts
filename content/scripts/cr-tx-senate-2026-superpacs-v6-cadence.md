## Scene 1 — Hook

**VO:** If you live in Texas, you couldn't turn on a TV this spring without an ad telling you how to vote. Here's who actually paid for them. Over one hundred ten million dollars tried to pick your next Texas senator. [pause:400ms] And the money lost. Picture an ad on your screen, paid for by "Texans for a Conservative Majority." The name sounds like a stadium full of your neighbors. It was not. It was a handful of billionaires. And the proof of who they are is sitting in a public file, free, right now. I will open it for you.

## Scene 2 — Stakes

**VO:** [pause:800ms] Here is the race. In 2026, Texas picks a new United States senator. That one person gets a vote on the laws that touch your whole life. First, each party holds a primary. [pause:300ms] In other words, the party picks its one name before November. And around those two primaries, the money came in like a flood. Over one hundred ten million dollars. The most of any Senate race in the country this year. Now stay with me. Because the biggest checks in this whole flood did something almost funny. They lost. I will show you the first one.

## Scene 3 — Receipt one (the backfire)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] Look at that first name again. Texans for a Conservative Majority. It is a super PAC. [pause:300ms] In other words, a group that can spend all the money it wants on ads. But it cannot talk to the candidate. And the candidate cannot control it. This one was built to protect John Cornyn, [pause:200ms] the senator who already had the job. The biggest check inside came from a Houston billionaire named John Nau. [pause:200ms] He sells beer for a living. And here is a detail that matters. He used to be Cornyn's money man — the person who raised cash for him. So of course he wanted Cornyn to keep the seat. He put in seven point nine million dollars. And his group spent thirty-nine million dollars trying to take down one rival. Ken Paxton. [pause:200ms] So here is the punchline. [pause:600ms] They spent thirty-nine million dollars to make Paxton lose. He won. [pause:1s] Sixty-four percent. The richest, loudest bet in Texas just went bust. And here is the thing. The other party did the very same thing.

## Scene 4 — Receipt two (the same trick, the other side)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] Walk across the aisle with me. There is a group called Lone Star Rising. It sounds like proud Texas folks at a barbecue. It was really money to lift up James Talarico, [pause:200ms] the Democrat who won his primary. It spent almost nine million dollars on ads against his rival, Jasmine Crockett. [pause:200ms] And one of the names behind it is Reid Hoffman. [pause:200ms] He co-founded LinkedIn. He lives in California, not Texas. He put in one point five million dollars. Now here is the part that makes people sit up. Talarico runs against this exact kind of money. He says super PACs should be against the law. [pause:400ms] And he won with a super PAC spending nine million dollars to clear his path. He has an answer. He says he will not fight with one hand tied behind his back while the other side spends freely. That is his answer. Is it a problem, or just the rules everyone plays by? You can decide. But there is one more layer. And it is the strangest one.

## Scene 5 — Receipt three (the disguise is the story)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] So who is really behind these friendly names? Here is the strangest part. Sometimes you cannot tell. And that is not an accident. Some of the money does not come straight from a person. It runs through a second group first. That trick has a name. A pass-through. [pause:300ms] In other words, the money slips through a middle group, so the paperwork only shows the middle name. Not the real person who paid. On the Cornyn side, five and a half million dollars came through a group called "Ohio Works." [pause:250ms] On the Talarico side, three and three-quarter million dollars came through one called "Government That Works." The real donors? The law lets them stay secret. We chased the trail as far as it goes. Then it goes dark. So we will stop there. We will not name a person we cannot see. And that is the whole game. The receipt always names the messenger. [pause:400ms] It almost never names the sender. That is not a mistake. That is the design.

## Scene 6 — Why the names matter (the deeper why)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] Now ask the real question. Why does a billionaire in California care who Texas sends to Washington? Texas is not the prize. The vote is. He runs tech companies. Washington writes the rules for tech companies. A senator gets a vote on those rules. Here is the honest twist. Back home in the state house, Talarico actually wrote bills to put more rules on social media and on fake A-I videos. [pause:300ms] That is not the easy ride Silicon Valley usually wants. On crypto — the new kind of digital money — he leans friendlier. But he has never cast a single Senate vote. So no one knows yet how he would vote on the big tech rules in Washington. And that is the thing to watch. A tech billionaire is helping pay for a seat that will one day vote on his own business. You get one vote. He gets to spend until the ads never stop. That is the gap this race is really about. We do not know what he gets back. We only know he is paying.

## Scene 7 — Verdict + CTA

**VO:** [pause:800ms] So here is what this race taught us. The money was louder than ever. The names were friendlier than ever. And in both primaries, the voters did the opposite of what the big money paid for. Thirty-nine million dollars could not stop Paxton. And the man who runs against super PACs beat his rival with one. Your one ballot still beat the checkbook. The names on the ads are a costume. The receipt under them is real. It is public. It is free to read. [pause:300ms] The full receipt and the newsletter are linked below. [pause:1.2s]
