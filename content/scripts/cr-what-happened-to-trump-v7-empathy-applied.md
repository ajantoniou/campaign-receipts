## BEAT 1 — You remember this voice (0:00–0:30)

[0:00–0:15 — Trump's own 2015 voice on a black frame. Date stamp lower third. No narrator. No music.]

[0:15–0:18 — hard cut to silence. White text on black: **June 21, 2025. Seven B-2s. Three sites. About 25 minutes.** Hold 3 seconds.]

**VO (0:18):** Seven planes. One page. Five months between them.

You voted on the page. The page is gone.

I'm going to show you the page. Then I'll show you what flew.

This is not a "we told you so." This is a receipt.

---

## BEAT 2 — He said it again in 2024, in writing (0:30–1:45)

[pause:800ms] You remember the voice. Nine years ago, he said the wars were a scam. A lot of people voted for him because of that. You may have been one of them.

Then 2024 came. He said it again. This time in writing.

It was on his campaign website. The section was called "Renew American Strength."

[On screen: verbatim block, audit-document type.]

The page said this. "President Trump will defend America against all threats, protect America against all dangers, and keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars."

Read that last part again. **Keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars.**

There was a second page. It was called the 20 Core Promises. Number 8 said this. "Prevent world war three. Restore peace in Europe and in the Middle East."

Two pages. [pause:400ms] Two promises. [pause:400ms] Same cycle. [pause:400ms] Same deal as 2015.

You read the page. You voted on the page. A lot of other people did too.

Hold that page in your head. The website. The line about wars.

In a minute, it won't be there.

---

## BEAT 3 — On January 20, 2025, the page came down (1:45–3:00)

[pause:800ms] He was sworn in on January 20, 2025.

That same day, donaldjtrump.com changed.

Not edited. [pause:400ms] Not moved. [pause:400ms] Not put in an old folder.

[pause:600ms] Gone. [pause:1000ms]

The policy page came down. The 20 Core Promises came down. The line about wars came down.

What was left was a donation form.

Go look. The words aren't there. The campaign site does not remember.

There is one place those words still live. It is free. It is called the Wayback Machine. [pause:200ms] It is a nonprofit library in San Francisco. [pause:200ms] Since 1996, it has saved pictures of websites. The internet has a memory that the websites don't.

The Wayback Machine remembers the page. The campaign site does not.

That is the first thing to hold. The deal you voted on now lives in an old picture.

The second thing is what happened five months later.

Hold the date in your head. January 20. The page came down.

Count five months forward.

---

## BEAT 4 — Five months later, the planes flew (3:00–4:30)

[pause:800ms] June 21, 2025.

Seven B-2 stealth bombers took off from a base in Missouri. They flew to Iran. They dropped fourteen bunker-busters on three sites. The sites were Fordow, [pause:200ms] Natanz, [pause:200ms] and Isfahan. [pause:200ms] The three places where Iran made nuclear fuel. A U.S. submarine fired Tomahawk missiles at the same time.

Start to finish, about twenty-five minutes.

The strike had a name. The Pentagon called it Operation Midnight Hammer.

That night, around ten o'clock, the President spoke from the White House. He called the strike "a spectacular military success." He said the sites had been "completely and totally obliterated."

The Secretary of Defense spoke the next morning. He said the strike was "very narrowly tailored." He said it was "necessary."

Now look at the two pages side by side.

The campaign page said keep America out of **unnecessary** foreign wars.

[pause:600ms] The strike was called **necessary**. [pause:1000ms]

The same person wrote the page. The same person ordered the strike.

You may have voted for him because of that page. A lot of people did. What I'm about to show you isn't about your vote being wrong. It is about the page being gone.

So was this a one-time thing? One hard week?

The 2016 checklist already graded this one. The grade is going to surprise you.

## BEAT 5 — The 2016 checklist already graded this (4:30–6:30)

[pause:800ms] There is a book called SEALED 2016. It is a checklist of his first term. One hundred forty-five promises. Same grading for each one.

The math is on the cover. Forty-six kept. [pause:250ms] Fifty-one partly kept. [pause:250ms] Forty broken. [pause:250ms] Eight up to the reader.

Two promises in that book matter for what you just saw.

Promise number 46. "Tear up the Iran nuclear deal." Grade: **KEPT.**

In May 2018, the President signed a paper. He pulled the United States out of a deal with Iran. The deal had one job. It was built to keep Iran from making a nuclear bomb. Iran agreed to let inspectors walk through the buildings. As long as Iran stayed in the deal, there was no reason to drop bombs.

He promised to tear up the deal. He tore it up. KEPT.

Promise number 51. "No more endless wars." Grade: **PARTLY KEPT.**

Now stay with me.

The kept promise tore up the deal. The deal was the thing that made bombing Iran not needed.

The partly kept promise was the one that said no more wars.

Both grades are in the same book.

You could call that a broken promise. A lot of people have. He kept one promise. [pause:600ms] The kept promise broke the other promise. [pause:1000ms]

He took away the off-switch. The inspectors walked out of the buildings. Five years later, he turned on the bombs.

You don't have to flip your vote to see that. You only have to read both pages.

So that is the Iran page. One page. One strike. Graded.

One Iran strike could be a one-time thing. So count the judges.

## BEAT 6 — The same shape on a second page (6:30–9:30)

[pause:800ms] Number 9 on the 20 Core Promises said this. "End the weaponization of government against the American people."

Stop using the government to punish people for speaking up.

A lot of people voted for that line. It was one of the biggest lines on the site.

Here is what the first few months looked like.

March 8, 2025. Mahmoud Khalil. [pause:200ms] He was a graduate student at Columbia. He had a green card. Federal agents took him from his apartment in New York. No warrant. They flew him to a jail in Louisiana. He sat there for one hundred and four days.

A federal judge ordered him released on June 20. The judge named the First Amendment.

March 25, 2025. Rumeysa Ozturk. [pause:200ms] She was a Ph.D. student at Tufts. Federal agents took her off a sidewalk in Massachusetts. The reason was an opinion piece she had written for the school paper.

A federal judge in Vermont ordered her released on May 9. An immigration judge later ended the case.

April 14, 2025. Mohsen Mahdawi. [pause:200ms] He was a Columbia student with a green card. He went to his U.S. citizenship interview in Vermont. Federal agents took him at the interview itself.

A federal judge ordered him released two weeks later. The judge wrote that the case raised "substantial claims" of "retaliation for protected speech."

March 2025. Badar Khan Suri. [pause:200ms] He was a researcher at Georgetown. He had a valid visa. Federal agents took him from outside his home in Virginia. They moved him to Texas. He sat there about two months.

A federal judge in Virginia ordered him released on May 14. The order named the First Amendment and the Fifth Amendment.

Now count.

Four people. [pause:400ms] Four cases. [pause:400ms] Four federal judges. [pause:400ms] Four times a judge had to write the words "First Amendment" before the door opened.

The page said end it. The first months ran it.

One Iran strike could be one-time. Four students, four judges, four First Amendment orders is not one-time.

Same shape as the Iran page. Different subject. Same shape.

## BEAT 7 — You voted on a page. The page is gone. (9:30–11:00)

[On screen: the donaldjtrump.com 2024 capture of the "keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars" line. Hold 4 seconds in silence. Then overlay: **PAGE DELETED — JAN 20, 2025**. Cut to black.]

[pause:800ms] So here is where we are.

You voted on a page. The page is gone. The grade is yours.

Nobody is telling you to flip. The page was the deal you were offered. You read it. You voted on it. That part is on paper. It was done in good faith.

Here is what is also on paper. The page came down on day one. The planes flew in month five. The four students sat in jail. The 2016 book already graded the kept promise that took the off-switch away.

You are allowed to hold both of those at the same time. You voted in good faith, AND the page is gone. The first part is about you. The second part is about him.

There is one small thing you can do before the next election. It costs nothing. It takes five seconds.

Before you vote, go to the candidate's website. Find the page you would be voting on. Save it. Screenshot it. Send it to yourself in an email.

The Wayback Machine is free, and it remembers. But you don't need the Wayback Machine if you saved the page yourself.

The campaign site is not built to remember. You are.

Next time, save the page before you vote.

If you want the rest of the pages, they are in a book. Sealed2016.com. [pause:1200ms] One hundred forty-five promises. Graded.

[On screen: SEALED2016.COM held in audit-document type. 5–8s music-bed-only tail. Fade.]
