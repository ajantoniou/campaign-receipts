## Scene 1 — The news, and the punchline

**VO:** This was the most expensive Senate race in the country this year. One hundred ten million dollars in outside money poured into one Texas seat. Most of it came from a handful of billionaires. [pause:400ms] Here is the part that matters. The biggest checks lost. One super PAC spent thirty-nine point three million dollars to beat a Republican named Ken Paxton. Paxton won anyway, with about sixty-four percent of the vote. On the other side, a super PAC spent almost nine million dollars to help a Democrat named James Talarico — a man who campaigns to ban super PACs. Talarico won too. [pause:400ms] So the money lost, and the voters did not bend. Now let us walk the receipts, because they are public, they are free, and they show exactly who paid and why.

## Scene 2 — How a Senate race works, and where the money sits

**VO:** [pause:600ms] Start with the basics. In 2026, Texas picks a new United States senator. That one person gets a vote on the laws that reach into your whole life. Before the general election, each party holds a primary. In other words, each party picks its one name first. Around those primaries, the money came in like a flood — more than one hundred ten million dollars, more than any other Senate race in the country. Most of that money did not come from the candidates. It came from super PACs. A super PAC is a group that can spend unlimited money on ads for a candidate, but is not allowed to coordinate with them — and the candidate does not control it. So the real story here is not the candidates. It is the people writing the checks.

## Scene 3 — John Nau: the thirty-nine-million-dollar bet that lost

**VO:** [pause:600ms] Start with the biggest check writer on the Republican side. His name is John Nau. He lives in Houston, and he is a billionaire who built the largest Anheuser-Busch beer distributor in the country. FEC filings show he gave seven point nine million dollars to a super PAC called "Texans for a Conservative Majority." That group was pro-Cornyn — it backed Senator John Cornyn, the Republican who already held the seat. [pause:300ms] Why would John Nau care this much? The record answers it plainly. Nau was Cornyn's national finance chairman — the man who raised money for him for years. He is part of the established, business-side of the Texas Republican Party, and he wanted his man to keep the seat. So his super PAC spent ten point six million dollars for Cornyn, and it spent thirty-nine point three million dollars against Cornyn's biggest rival — Ken Paxton — to drive Paxton down. [pause:400ms] It did not work. Paxton won the primary with about sixty-four percent. The single largest wall of money in the race was built to stop one man, and that man won anyway.

## Scene 4 — Reid Hoffman: a California check in a Texas race, and the reason

**VO:** [pause:600ms] Now cross to the Democratic side, where the pattern repeats. One of the donors is Reid Hoffman. He co-founded LinkedIn, the big jobs website, and he is a tech billionaire who lives in California — not Texas. FEC filings show he gave one point five million dollars to a super PAC called "Lone Star Rising." That group backed James Talarico, the Democrat who won his primary. It spent seven point nine six million dollars for Talarico and eight point nine five million dollars against his rival, Jasmine Crockett. [pause:300ms] So why would a California tech billionaire spend on a Texas Senate seat? Here is the documented reason. Hoffman runs tech companies, and Washington makes the rules for tech companies — a senator gets a vote on those rules. He also has a long record of spending in Democratic primaries to lift the more moderate candidate over the more progressive one, which is exactly what this PAC did against Crockett. [pause:400ms] There is an honest complication, and we are going to keep it honest. Talarico's record is not simply pro-tech. As a Texas state lawmaker, he wrote bills to put more rules on social media and to crack down on fake A-I videos — that is tougher on Silicon Valley, not softer. And Talarico has never cast a single Senate vote, so no one yet knows how he would vote on the big tech rules in Washington. We know Hoffman is paying. What he gets back is the open question to watch.

## Scene 5 — The disguised money: when the receipt hides the sender

**VO:** [pause:600ms] Some of the biggest checks in this race have no name you can read. That is not a gap in our reporting — it is the design. Part of the money did not come straight from a person. It ran through a second group first, so the paperwork shows only the middle group's name, not the person who really paid. That is called a pass-through, and the law allows it. [pause:300ms] On the Republican side, five and a half million dollars came into the pro-Cornyn PAC through a group called "Ohio Works." On the Democratic side, three and three-quarter million dollars came into the pro-Talarico PAC through a group called "Government That Works." Both groups are allowed by law to keep their original donors secret. We followed the trail as far as the public records go, and then it goes dark, so we stop — we will not name a person we cannot verify. [pause:400ms] Both sides did this. The receipt names the messenger. It does not name the sender. That is legal, and it is on purpose.

## Scene 6 — What the receipts add up to

**VO:** [pause:600ms] Put it back together. This was the most expensive Senate race in the country. A Texas beer billionaire helped build a thirty-nine-million-dollar wall of money to beat Ken Paxton, and Paxton won with about sixty-four percent. A California tech billionaire reached across the country to help James Talarico, and Talarico won — even though Talarico campaigns to ban the very kind of super PAC that helped him. [pause:300ms] Talarico has an answer for that tension. He says he will not disarm by himself while the other side keeps using super PACs, and that he welcomes donors who believe their own influence should be limited. We report his answer and let you judge it. [pause:300ms] The names on these groups were friendly — "Texans for a Conservative Majority," "Lone Star Rising," "Government That Works." The checks behind them were not friendly. They were enormous. And in both primaries, the voters chose the opposite of what the biggest money paid for.
