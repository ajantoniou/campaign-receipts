## Flags

### FLAG-01 — ASSUMES_CONTEXT_VIEWER_DOESNT_HAVE
**Line(s):** Line 90 — "There is one place those words still live. It is free. It is called the Wayback Machine. It takes pictures of websites."
**Why it breaks empathy:** Cincinnati Mom has never heard of the Wayback Machine. The script names it and defines it in the same breath — good — but it does not anchor WHO runs it or WHY it exists, so it can read as "a thing the narrator knows about that you don't." A single human-side anchor ("a nonprofit library in San Francisco that's been saving web pages since 1996") would put it on the same shelf as a real-world library card.
**Suggested re-frame (advisory only):** Add one sentence naming it as a free public library for the internet, run by a nonprofit, so the viewer trusts the source instead of taking the narrator's word.

### FLAG-02 — ASSUMES_CONTEXT_VIEWER_DOESNT_HAVE
**Line(s):** Line 108 — "They dropped fourteen bunker-busters on three sites. The sites were Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan."
**Why it breaks empathy:** Three Iranian place names land cold for a viewer who has never followed the nuclear file. The names are correct and load-bearing, but without a one-clause anchor ("the three places where Iran enriched uranium"), Cincinnati Mom hears three foreign words and tunes out for the next sentence.
**Suggested re-frame (advisory only):** Add a six-word anchor after the list — "the three places Iran made nuclear fuel" — so the names mean something instead of being trivia.

### FLAG-03 — ASSUMES_CONTEXT_VIEWER_DOESNT_HAVE
**Line(s):** Line 144 — "In May 2018, the President signed a paper. He pulled the United States out of a deal with Iran. The deal had one job. It was built to keep Iran from making a nuclear bomb."
**Why it breaks empathy:** This is actually clean — "the deal had one job" is the bedtime-story anchor working. The only soft spot is that the viewer is never told the other side of the deal: what Iran gave up in return. Without that, "off-switch" in line 160 has no mechanical meaning. One sentence ("Iran agreed to let inspectors walk through the buildings") makes the off-switch literal instead of metaphorical.
**Suggested re-frame (advisory only):** One added sentence on what Iran traded away (inspectors in the buildings) — turns the off-switch from a figure of speech into a concrete object.

### FLAG-04 — META_COMMENTARY (minor)

**Suggested re-frame (advisory only):** Either keep as-is (bedtime cadence) or swap to "Here is the line that matters." — same beat, no instruction.

### FLAG-05 — META_COMMENTARY (minor)

**Suggested re-frame (advisory only):** Delete the aside and let the next date carry the beat ("April 14, 2025. Mohsen Mahdawi.").

### FLAG-06 — TONE (adjacent — not rage, but sharpness)
**Line(s):** Line 158 — "You could call that a lie. A lot of people have."
**Why it breaks empathy:** Not a rage leak — it's hedged into third person — but "lie" is the sharpest word in the script and it sits next to the voter's own choice. The disappointment-not-rage lock holds because the narrator does NOT say "he lied to you," but a Trump-2024 voter may still feel cornered by the proximity of "lie" + "you voted." A softer noun ("a broken promise," already in the script's vocabulary) keeps the receipt and removes the sting.
**Suggested re-frame (advisory only):** "You could call that a broken promise. A lot of people have." — same fact, no flinch.
