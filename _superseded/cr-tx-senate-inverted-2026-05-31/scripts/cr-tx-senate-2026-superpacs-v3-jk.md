## Scene 1 — Hook (story-question cold open)
**VO:** "Picture yourself on the couch in Texas. An ad comes on. It says it is from Texans for a Conservative Majority. Sounds like your neighbors, right? Now pull one thread. Who actually paid for that ad? Here is what we found. Nearly ninety million dollars is already picking your next senator. And most of it was spent before you voted."

## Scene 2 — Stakes / context
**VO:** "This is the race for one Texas seat in the U.S. Senate. Right now it is the most expensive Senate race in the country. The strange part is the money. Most of it does not come from the people running. It comes from outside groups called super PACs. In other words, a super PAC raises huge checks and buys ads. It just can not hand that money to a campaign. So the names on the ads are not the candidates. They are the groups. And every group picked a name that sounds like you. So stay with me, and let's pull the thread. Who is really behind the friendly name?"

## Scene 3 — The name game (Receipt 1, the big reveal)
**VO:** "Start with the biggest one. Texans for a Conservative Majority. The name sounds like a room full of neighbors. So we pulled its filing. Every super PAC must tell the government who pays it. That filing is public. It is free to read. And here is the part most people miss. The crowd is not a crowd. It is mostly one man. His name is John Nau. He sells beer. He is a billionaire. He put in about fifteen point six million dollars. A Wall Street boss named Stephen Schwarzman added four million more. So a name that says Texans is really three rich men. That group has spent twenty-four million dollars. All of it against one Republican, Ken Paxton."

## Scene 4 — The pattern (Receipt 2)
**VO:** "Then you pull the next thread. And it happens again. A group called Truth and Courage has spent about twenty-two million dollars. All of it against the Democrat, Colin Allred. Behind the warm name is mostly one out-of-state billionaire, Jeff Yass. Same trick. A friendly name on the outside. One giant check on the inside."

## Scene 5 — Mid turn: not just one party (Receipt 3)
**VO:** "Now you might be thinking only one side does this. So did we. We were wrong. Look at a group called Lone Star Rising. It sounds like proud Texas folks. But the money is liberal money. A lot of it comes from a tech billionaire in California named Reid Hoffman. And it spends against a Democrat, Jasmine Crockett, inside the Democrats own race. So both sides wear the costume. That is the real story."

## Scene 6 — Why it matters (voter pivot)
**VO:** "Stop and think about who this is for. Ken Paxton and Colin Allred want to speak for thirty million Texans. And the loudest voices in their race are not Texans. They are a few billionaires you never met. They are spending money you were never meant to trace."

## Scene 7 — The hidden ones (honest gap)
**VO:** "And some threads still do not pull all the way. One group, WinSenate, spent ten million dollars to help Allred. But its filing does not name its donors yet. Another group spent almost five million against Wesley Hunt. All of it came through one out-of-state pass-through. So here is the honest thing. We do not know those names yet. The filing does not show them. And we will not make a name up."

## Scene 8 — Verdict (the costume turn)
**VO:** "So here is the punchline. The friendly names are a costume. Texans for a Conservative Majority is one beer billionaire. Truth and Courage is one hedge-fund billionaire. The warm name is the disguise. It is not the message. And the real face is written in only one place. The public filing."

## Scene 9 — Cold close
**VO:** "Here is what this race taught us. The next ad will sound like your neighbors. It will not be. But the receipt that names who really paid is public. It is free. And it is yours. The full receipt and our free newsletter are linked below."
