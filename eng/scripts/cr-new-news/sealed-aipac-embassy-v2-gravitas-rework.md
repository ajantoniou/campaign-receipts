## DECISION

**The free-speech-sacrifice / war-dead frame does NOT fit this piece. I deepened the existing gravitas instead — the gravity of the public record itself.**

### Why

The founder's free-speech-sacrifice motif ("Americans died to win the freedom to speak") is an honest fit for the **campus free-speech sister piece**. It is NOT an honest fit here. This piece's true subject is a kept promise that shares a date with sixty deaths at the Gaza border, and a viewer's right to see the whole record.

Bolting a "Americans died for free speech" theme onto a Gaza-border death-toll story would borrow gravity from one set of dead to dramatize another. That directly violates the **death-toll care lock** — it would weaponize the sixty dead by association and tell the viewer how to feel. It would also feel grafted-on: there is no documented link between the embassy/Gaza date and any free-speech war.

The honest gravitas this specific piece can carry — and now carries more of — is **the public record itself**: dates, counts, and signed papers are written down on purpose, so power cannot quietly show people only half. The right to see the whole afternoon is the thing worth defending. That is restrained, true to the facts, and it strengthens (rather than competes with) the existing "you deserve the whole afternoon" weight and the seriousness of real people on a real day. It also harmonizes thematically with the sister piece's "what the freedom cost" frame without faking a war/speech link.
