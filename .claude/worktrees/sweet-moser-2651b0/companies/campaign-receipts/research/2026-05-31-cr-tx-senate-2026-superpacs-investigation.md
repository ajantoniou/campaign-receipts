## Thread 1 — Reid Hoffman → Lone Star Rising PAC → James Talarico — $1.5M (FEC C00918268)

**WHO they really are.**
Reid Hoffman — co-founded LinkedIn, partner at the Silicon Valley venture firm Greylock,
a California tech billionaire. [FACT] He is one of the largest Democratic megadonors in
the country, having given at least ~$34.8M across recent cycles, ranking among the top 30
U.S. donors. [FACT] His public politics are explicitly tech-industry-defensive: in January
2026 he urged Silicon Valley leaders to "stop bending the knee to Trump," and he has said
the Democratic Party "really did alienate" the tech world with "attacks on crypto" and
"attacks on Big Tech." [FACT]

**WHY they're here (the question this race answers for him).**
He lives in California. This is a Texas U.S. Senate seat. Tech money does not usually flow
into a *Texas Democratic primary* — so the gap itself is the lede. [FACT — geography of
the $1.5M is on the FEC Schedule A.] But Hoffman has a *documented method* that explains it:
since 2022 he has bankrolled the "Mainstream Democrats PAC," spending millions to beat
*more progressive* Democrats in primaries and push moderates to November. [PATTERN] Lone
Star Rising did exactly that here — it spent **$8.95M AGAINST Jasmine Crockett** (the more
progressive, anti-corporate candidate) and **$7.96M FOR Talarico** (the moderate). [FACT,
FEC Sched-E] The buy fits the Hoffman playbook precisely: out-of-state tech money, deployed
in a primary, to stop the left-flank Democrat. The open thing isn't *whether* he does this —
it's *what he wants from the Senate seat.*

**The candidate's record / likely posture (what the donor is buying).**
Talarico, unlike a brand-new candidate, has a real tech record as a Texas state rep — and
it is **NOT** uniformly what Silicon Valley wants, which is the honest complication:
- [FACT] He authored Texas legislation to **regulate AI-generated political ads / deepfakes**
  and to make **social-media platforms build deepfake-reporting processes** — i.e., MORE
  platform regulation, not less. He says innovation "shouldn't come at the cost of children's
  safety" and criticizes "predatory algorithms."
- [FACT] On **crypto** he is industry-friendlier: he backs "clear, fair rules" and a state
  blockchain working group — a regulate-but-grow posture, closer to what crypto investors want.
- [FACT] He pushed pro-industry growth items: Texas Semiconductor Innovation Consortium,
  broadband infrastructure fund, IT apprenticeship credential.
- [OPEN] He has **no U.S. Senate voting record yet** (he's a state legislator). The federal
  questions Hoffman's industry cares about — Section 230, federal AI liability rules, Big-Tech
  antitrust, H-1B / high-skill immigration caps — are **not yet on Talarico's record at the
  federal level.** Frame as the watch-for: a tech megadonor is buying a future Senate vote on
  rules that govern his own companies, and the candidate's *state* record is mixed (tough on
  platforms/AI ads, soft on crypto). Will the Senate version vote the tech-industry way?
  Unanswered — invite the viewer to watch, do not assert.

**The historical pattern.**
[PATTERN] This is not a one-off. Hoffman's Mainstream Democrats PAC has, since 2022, repeatedly
spent to beat progressives in Democratic primaries (e.g., backing Henry Cuellar against a
progressive challenger in Texas in 2022). The pro-Talarico / anti-Crockett split is the same
strategy in a bigger race. The proof it's a strategy, not a whim, is the repetition.

**The deeper story in one honest line (for the storyteller, NOT a punchline).**
A California tech billionaire reached across the country into a Texas Democratic primary to
help beat the more progressive candidate — the same move he has made before — and the seat he's
investing in will one day vote on the rules that govern his own industry.

## Thread 2 — John L. Nau III → Texans for a Conservative Majority → pro-Cornyn / anti-Paxton — $7.9M (FEC C00542217)

**WHO they really are.**
John L. Nau III — chairman/CEO of Silver Eagle Beverages, the largest distributor of
Anheuser-Busch products in the United States; a Houston-based billionaire. [FACT] He is a
pillar of the *establishment* Texas GOP donor class: he served as **national finance chairman
for John Cornyn**, and is a top donor to the Associated Republicans of Texas (the
establishment-backed group). His giving even crosses the aisle modestly (>$85K to Democrats).
[FACT]

**WHY they're here (the question this race answers for him).**
This was an intra-Republican civil war: establishment-business GOP vs. MAGA insurgent. Nau
put **$7,905,000** into Texans for a Conservative Majority, the pro-Cornyn vehicle that spent
**$39.3M AGAINST Ken Paxton** to stop him and **$10.6M FOR Cornyn.** [FACT, FEC Sched-A/E]
Nau isn't an out-of-industry stranger like Hoffman — he's the *embodiment* of the
business-Republican wing trying to keep the seat with their guy. The "why" is factional and
economic: a regulated-distribution billionaire (alcohol is one of the most state-regulated
industries in America) backing the predictable, business-friendly incumbent over an
unpredictable insurgent.

**The candidate's record / likely posture (what the donor is buying).**
[FACT] Cornyn is the business establishment's senator: his donor roster "resembles a Texas
boardroom," and on the tech/economic issues he is industry-accommodating — he supports the
GENIUS Act stablecoin framework, has publicly courted Texas Bitcoin mining, and three former
crypto lobbyists previously worked on his staff. He is the known, stable, pro-business vote.
[OPEN] What Nau's own industry (beverage distribution) wants from a senator — the
three-tier alcohol distribution system, tariffs on inputs, tax treatment — is not something
Cornyn has a single signature vote on that we sourced here; frame as the industry-interest
backdrop, not a claimed transaction.
The irony for the storyteller: Cornyn was the *safe, business-friendly* choice — and the
$39.3M spent to stop Paxton **lost.** Paxton won 63.8%.

**The historical pattern.**
[PATTERN] Nau's role isn't new — he was Cornyn's finance chairman and a long-time
establishment-GOP financier. Establishment business money backing the incumbent against a
right-flank insurgent is the recurring shape of modern GOP primaries; here the insurgent won
anyway, which is the larger pattern story (big establishment money losing to MAGA energy).

**The deeper story in one honest line (for the storyteller, NOT a punchline).**
A Houston beer-distribution billionaire — the establishment GOP's banker — spent nearly $8M of
a $39M wall of money to stop the insurgent and protect the business-friendly incumbent, and the
insurgent won anyway.

## Thread 3 — The pass-throughs: "Ohio Works Inc." and "Government That Works PAC" — the disguise IS the story

**WHO they really are.**
Two of the largest single deposits into these PACs came from groups whose own donors are one
filing removed and therefore **unnameable**:
- **Ohio Works Inc.** routed ~$5.55M into the pro-Cornyn Texans for a Conservative Majority.
  Original donors not itemized. [FACT — per sources.md; we deliberately do not name a person.]
- **Government That Works PAC** gave **$3.75M** to the pro-Talarico Lone Star Rising — the single
  biggest check that PAC received — and that money "largely came from undisclosed sources." [FACT]
  Reporting traces $4M into Government That Works from the **Sixteen Thirty Fund**, a top
  Democratic dark-money hub that is not required to report its donors, right around the time it
  cut the $3.75M to Lone Star Rising. [FACT, Texas Tribune] So the trail goes:
  *unknown donors → Sixteen Thirty Fund → Government That Works → Lone Star Rising → anti-Crockett
  ads.* The face on the last receipt is a friendly PAC name; the first name in the chain is blank.

**WHY they're here (the question this race answers for them).**
The whole point is that we *cannot answer it* — and that opacity is the product, not a gap in
our research. The money was structured so a Texas voter watching the ad cannot see who paid for
it. Both sides did it (Ohio Works on the GOP side, Government That Works / Sixteen Thirty on the
Dem side), so this is both-sides by construction.

**The candidate's record / likely posture.**
Not applicable — no named human to attach a record to. That is the entire finding: a tens-of-
millions slice of a $110M race has **no visible author.** [FACT/OPEN]

**The historical pattern.**
[PATTERN] One-filing-removed pass-throughs (a PAC giving to a PAC, or a 501(c)(4) "social welfare"
nonprofit funding a super PAC) are the standard modern method for spending big while hiding the
origin. Sixteen Thirty Fund is a recurring name in exactly this role cycle after cycle.

**The deeper story in one honest line (for the storyteller, NOT a punchline).**
On both sides, millions arrived through groups with patriotic names whose real donors are hidden
one layer up — the receipt names the messenger, never the sender, and that is by design.

## Thread 4 — The irony thread (sourced, framed as a question — NOT a verdict)

**WHO / WHAT.**
James Talarico's central campaign message is *anti-billionaire and anti-super-PAC.* [FACT] He
has sworn off corporate PAC money, proposed cracking down on "billionaire tax loopholes," and
explicitly **advocated outlawing super PACs.** His campaign says 97% of his ~$40M *campaign-account*
haul came from ~290,000 small-dollar donors. [FACT]

**WHY it's a story.**
Meanwhile a *separate* super PAC — Lone Star Rising — spent ~$9M of billionaire money
(Hoffman, Connecticut hedge-funder Stephen Mandel, Austin philanthropist Simone Coxe, plus the
dark-money pass-through) to help him win by attacking Crockett. [FACT] He won the primary 52.4%.
[FACT]

**The candidate's own answer (in his words — important for fairness).**
[FACT] Asked about this, Talarico said he will not "unilaterally disarm while Republicans play by
their own rules," and that he welcomes billionaire supporters who *believe they should be taxed
more and have their political influence limited.* Include his rebuttal — it keeps this an honest
question, not a gotcha.

**The deeper story in one honest line (for the storyteller, NOT a punchline).**
The candidate who wants to outlaw super PACs won his primary with the help of one — a tension he
answers by saying he won't disarm alone. State the tension; let the viewer judge; do not call it
hypocrisy as a verdict.

## What the storyteller must simplify (3rd-grade, literal, no metaphors)

- **"Super PAC"** → "a group that spends unlimited money on ads for a candidate, but isn't allowed
  to coordinate with them — and the candidate doesn't control it."
- **"Pass-through" / "one filing removed"** → "money that goes through a second group first, so the
  paperwork only shows the middle group's name, not the real person who paid."
- **"Dark money" / "Sixteen Thirty Fund"** → "a group that is allowed by law to keep its donors
  secret." Don't name a person — the point is we CAN'T.
- **"Schedule A / Schedule E"** → don't say these on screen; just "the public records show…"
- **"Section 230 / federal AI liability / antitrust"** → "the rules in Washington about what tech
  companies can do and what they can be sued for." Keep it one idea.
- **"H-1B / high-skill immigration"** → "the visas that let tech companies hire workers from
  other countries."
- **"GENIUS Act / stablecoin framework"** → "a new law setting rules for digital money (crypto)."
- **"Three-tier alcohol distribution"** → skip entirely; just "his business is heavily regulated
  by the government."
- **Hoffman's industry posture** → "He runs tech companies in California. Washington makes the
  rules for tech companies. A senator gets a vote on those rules." Keep that chain literal.
- **The committee FEC ID numbers** → on-screen Remotion only; never spoken.
- **Open questions stay questions.** When the storyteller reaches Talarico's federal posture or
  what Nau's industry wants, the line must end as a question the viewer watches for — never an
  answer. Guardrail A applies to the storyteller too.
