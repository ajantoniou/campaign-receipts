**VO:** [pause:800ms] First, the basics. In 2026, Texas picks a new United States senator. That one person gets a vote on the laws that touch your whole life. ## Scene 3 — Billionaire one: John Nau (the $39 million backfire)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] First up is a billionaire from Texas. His name is John Nau. He lives in Houston, and he sells beer for a living. He put seven point nine million dollars into a group with a friendly name: "Texans for a Conservative Majority." [pause:300ms] That name sounds like a stadium full of your neighbors. It was not. It was mostly him. The group is a super PAC. [pause:200ms] In other words, it can spend all the money it wants on ads. But it cannot talk to the candidate, and the candidate cannot control it. So why would John Nau care so much? [pause:400ms] Here is the reason. He used to be John Cornyn's money man — the person who raised cash for him for years. Cornyn was the senator who already had the seat. Nau wanted his man to keep it. So his group spent thirty-nine point three million dollars trying to take down one rival. Ken Paxton. [pause:300ms] So here is the punchline. [pause:600ms] They spent thirty-nine point three million dollars to make Paxton lose. He won. [pause:1s] Sixty-four percent. The richest, loudest bet in Texas just went bust. Get it? Now let's cross to the other side. Because they did the very same thing.

## Scene 4 — Billionaire two: Reid Hoffman (the why that fits perfectly)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] Next up is a billionaire from California. His name is Reid Hoffman. He co-founded LinkedIn, the big work website. He does not live in Texas. He lives across the country. But he put one point five million dollars into a group called "Lone Star Rising." [pause:300ms] That name sounds like proud Texas folks at a barbecue. It was really money to lift up James Talarico, the Democrat who won his primary. The group spent almost nine million dollars on ads against his rival, Jasmine Crockett. So why would a California tech billionaire care who Texas sends to Washington? [pause:400ms] Here is where it gets interesting. Talarico campaigns to ban super PACs — the exact kind of group Hoffman just used to help him. And back in the state house, Talarico wrote bills to put more rules on social media and on fake A-I videos. [pause:300ms] That is tougher on Silicon Valley than most politicians dare to be. So a tech billionaire spending big to get close to that seat? No surprise. Washington writes the rules for tech companies. A senator gets a vote on those rules. [pause:300ms] But here is the honest part. Talarico has never cast a single Senate vote. So no one knows yet how he would vote on the big tech rules. That is the thing to watch. We know Hoffman is paying. We do not know what he gets back.

## Scene 5 — The disguise (the receipt names the messenger, never the sender)

**VO:** [pause:800ms] Now for the strangest layer of all. Sometimes you cannot even tell who paid. And that is not an accident. Some of the money does not come straight from a person. It runs through a second group first. That trick has a name. A pass-through. [pause:300ms] In other words, the money slips through a middle group, so the paperwork only shows the middle name — not the real person who paid. On the Cornyn side, five and a half million dollars came through a group called "Ohio Works." [pause:250ms] On the Talarico side, three and three-quarter million dollars came through one called "Government That Works." Who really sent that money? The law lets them stay secret. We chased the trail as far as it goes. Then it goes dark. So we stop there. We will not name a person we cannot see. And that is the whole game. The receipt always names the messenger. [pause:400ms] It almost never names the sender. That is not a mistake. That is the design.
