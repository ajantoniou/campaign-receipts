## HOOK

**VO:** Over one hundred ten million dollars tried to pick your next Texas senator. And it lost. A group called "Texans for a Conservative Majority" ran the ads. The name sounds like a crowd of neighbors. It was not. The receipt is public, and free, and I will show you who really paid.

## CONTEXT

**VO:** If you live in Texas, you saw the ads all spring. You did not pick who paid for them. This is the 2026 race for U.S. Senate. The seat that helps decide what laws get passed in Washington. Both parties had to choose a candidate first. That is called a primary. In other words, the party picks its one name before the big election in November. And around those two primaries, outside money poured in. Over one hundred ten million dollars. More than any other Senate race in the country this year. But here is the part most people miss. The biggest checks did not get what they paid for.

## RECEIPT ONE — THE BACKFIRE

**VO:** Start with the name on the ad. Texans for a Conservative Majority. It is a super PAC. In other words, a group that can spend unlimited money on ads for a candidate. But it is not allowed to talk to that candidate, and the candidate does not control it. This one was built to help John Cornyn, the sitting senator. The biggest single check inside it came from a Houston billionaire named John Nau. He sells beer. Seven point nine million dollars. And this group spent thirty-nine million dollars on ads against one man. Ken Paxton. So here is the punchline. They spent thirty-nine million dollars to stop Paxton. And on May 26th, Paxton won anyway. Sixty-four percent. The biggest checkbook in the race made a bet, and the bet lost.

## RECEIPT TWO — THE SAME TRICK, OTHER SIDE

**VO:** Now cross the aisle, because both sides did this. There is another group called Lone Star Rising. Sounds like proud Texas folks. It is pro-Talarico money. James Talarico is the Democrat who won his primary. This group spent almost nine million dollars on ads against his rival, Jasmine Crockett. One of the names behind it is Reid Hoffman. He co-founded LinkedIn. He lives in California, not Texas. He put in one point five million dollars. Here is the strange part. Talarico runs against this exact kind of money. He says super PACs should be against the law. And he won with a super PAC spending nine million dollars to clear his path. He answers that he will not fight with one hand tied while the other side spends freely. Fair enough. You can decide what you think.

## RECEIPT THREE — THE DISGUISE IS THE STORY

**VO:** So who is really behind these friendly names? Sometimes you cannot tell. And that is on purpose. Some of the money runs through a second group first. A pass-through. In other words, the money goes through a middle group, so the paperwork only shows the middle name, not the real person who paid. On the Cornyn side, five and a half million dollars came through a group called "Ohio Works Inc." On the Talarico side, three and three-quarter million dollars came through a group called "Government That Works." That money came from donors the law lets stay secret. We followed the trail as far as it goes. Then it goes dark. So we will not name a person we cannot see. That honesty is the whole point. The receipt names the messenger. It almost never names the sender.

## WHY THE NAMES MATTER

**VO:** Think about why a billionaire from California cares about a Texas seat. He runs tech companies. Washington makes the rules for tech companies. A senator gets a vote on those rules. Talarico has a record in the state house. He wrote bills to put more rules on social media and on fake A-I videos. That is not what Silicon Valley usually wants. On crypto, the new kind of digital money, he is friendlier to the industry. But he has never voted in the Senate. So nobody knows yet how he would vote on the big tech rules in Washington. That is the thing to watch. A tech billionaire is helping buy a seat that will one day vote on his own business. We do not know what he will get. We just know he is paying.

## VERDICT + CTA

**VO:** So here is what this race taught us. The money was louder than ever. The names were friendlier than ever. And in both primaries, the voters still did the opposite of what the big money paid for. Thirty-nine million dollars could not stop Paxton. And the candidate who runs against super PACs beat his rival with one. Your one ballot still beat the checkbook. The names on the ads are a costume.
