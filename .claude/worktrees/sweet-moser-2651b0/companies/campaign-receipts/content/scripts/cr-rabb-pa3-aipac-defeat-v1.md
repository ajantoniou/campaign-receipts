## VO script

A pediatric surgeon who ran the Black Doctors COVID Consortium just lost a Philadelphia primary by twenty points. The candidate who beat her is a state rep most of the country has never heard of. And the most interesting thing in this race isn't either of them — it's a foundation in Delaware that was ninety-one days old when it cut its first check.

On May 19th, Pennsylvania's Department of State certified the Third Congressional District primary. Chris Rabb, a state rep from Mount Airy, took forty-four percent. State Senator Sharif Street took thirty. Dr. Ala Stanford — the surgeon — took twenty-four. It's an open seat. Longtime Congressman Dwight Evans is retiring. Whoever wins this Democratic primary wins the seat. So this race was the race. And the money knew it.

Here's where it gets strange. On December 19th, 2025, a group called the Kimbark Foundation registered as a nonprofit in Delaware. Three months later, in March 2026, Kimbark wired five hundred thousand dollars to something called the 314 Action Fund — that's a federal political action committee. A P.A.C. In other words, an outside political group that can raise and spend unlimited money to influence a race. Kimbark made exactly two known gifts in its entire existence — that five hundred thousand to 314 Action Fund, and another five hundred thousand to a group called EDW Action Fund a few weeks earlier. Two gifts. One million dollars. Then nothing. You can pull the 314 Action Fund's filings yourself — F.E.C. committee C00633248.

Now, the 314 Action Fund brands itself as the P.A.C. that elects scientists to Congress. Lab coats. Beakers. Science. And once that five hundred thousand from Kimbark landed, 314 Action Fund spent three and a half million dollars on independent expenditures backing Dr. Stanford — the only scientist in the field. In other words, ad buys and mailers paid for directly by the P.A.C., never touching Stanford's campaign account. The F.E.C. breakdown: two-point-seven-five million on advertising, six hundred fifty-six thousand on direct mail, plus media production and text messages. Reporting from Drop Site News and Common Dreams traces the Kimbark shell back to donors aligned with A.I.P.A.C. — a major Israel-policy lobby in Washington. A.I.P.A.C. denies any connection. The receipt does not.

And here's the punchline you only see if you've been watching. This is the third time this channel has shown you the same playbook. In 2024, Cori Bush lost her Missouri primary after A.I.P.A.C.'s super-P.A.C., U.D.P., spent eight and a half million dollars against her. Last week, Kentucky Congressman Thomas Massie lost his primary after pro-Israel groups dropped fifteen-point-eight million — U.D.P., the R.J.C. Victory Fund, and an Adelson-linked transfer. Massie said it on camera: "ninety-five percent of my opponent's money came from the Israeli lobby." Bush, eight-point-five million, lost. Massie, fifteen-point-eight million, lost. Rabb, three-point-five million spent against him, won by fifteen points. Same playbook. Three outcomes. One variable.

What changed in Philly? On April 24th, A.O.C. endorsed Rabb — which put him on a national small-dollar fundraising list overnight. On April 30th, Hasan Piker showed up at Malcolm X Park in West Philly and canvassed for Rabb on a live Twitch stream. The money didn't stay invisible long enough to work.

Common Dreams ran the headline "A.I.P.A.C. Lost!" the day the result was certified. The verdict here is narrower and harder. A.I.P.A.C. didn't lose. The shell lost. The next shell will have a different name, a different state of incorporation, and a different month on the registration. F.E.C. committee C00633248 is the whole episode.
