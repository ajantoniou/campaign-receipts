## Scene 1 — The news, and the punchline

**VO:** This was the most expensive Senate race in the country this year. One hundred ten million dollars in outside money poured into one Texas seat. Most of it came from two billionaires. [pause:400ms] The biggest checks lost. One super PAC — a group that can spend unlimited money on ads — spent thirty-nine point three million dollars to beat a Republican named Ken Paxton. Paxton won anyway, with about sixty-four percent of the vote. [pause:400ms] So the money lost. Let us walk the receipts. They are public, and they show who paid and why.

## Scene 2 — John Nau: the thirty-nine-million-dollar bet that lost

**VO:** [pause:500ms] Start with the biggest check on the Republican side. His name is John Nau. He is a Houston beer billionaire. FEC filings show he gave seven point nine million dollars to a super PAC that backed Senator John Cornyn, the Republican who already held the seat. [pause:300ms] Why would Nau care this much? Nau was Cornyn's finance chairman — the man who raised money for him for years. So his PAC spent thirty-nine point three million dollars against Cornyn's biggest rival, Ken Paxton. [pause:300ms] It did not work. The largest wall of money in the race was built to stop one man, and that man won anyway.

## Scene 3 — Reid Hoffman: a California check in a Texas race

**VO:** [pause:500ms] Now cross to the Democratic side. One donor is Reid Hoffman. He co-founded LinkedIn, and he is a tech billionaire who lives in California — not Texas. FEC filings show he gave one point five million dollars to a super PAC called Lone Star Rising. That group backed James Talarico, and it spent eight point nine five million dollars against his rival, Jasmine Crockett. [pause:300ms] So why would a California tech billionaire spend on a Texas seat? As a state lawmaker, Talarico wrote bills to put rules on social media and on fake A-I videos — rules that reach Silicon Valley. A tech billionaire's interest in that race is no surprise. But Talarico has never cast a Senate vote, so what Hoffman gets back is still an open question.

## Scene 4 — The disguised money

**VO:** [pause:500ms] Some of this money is hidden on purpose. Five and a half million dollars reached the pro-Cornyn PAC through a shell group called Ohio Works. Three and three-quarter million dollars reached the pro-Talarico PAC through one called Government That Works. The original donors paid through these middle groups so their own names never appear. The law lets them stay secret. Both sides did it. The receipt shows the middle group, never the person who actually wrote the check.

## Scene 5 — What it means, and where to read it

**VO:** [pause:500ms] So here is what this race shows. The money was louder than ever, and the group names were friendlier than ever. But both winners beat the money that backed them. A billionaire can spend until the ads never stop, but he still gets only one vote. This year, the votes beat the checkbook on both sides. [pause:300ms] You can read the full receipt and get the newsletter at campaignreceipts.com. [pause:1.2s]
