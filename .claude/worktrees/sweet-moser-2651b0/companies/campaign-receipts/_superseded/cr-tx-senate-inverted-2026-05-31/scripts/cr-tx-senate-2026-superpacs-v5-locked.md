## Scene 1 — Hook (empathy ack + number + outcome)
**VO:** "If you live in Texas, you are tired of these ads. And you may have been told the money behind them is none of your business. Here is what we found. Nearly ninety million dollars is already picking your next senator, before you ever voted. Picture yourself on the couch. An ad comes on. It says it is from Texans for a Conservative Majority. Sounds like your neighbors, right? Now pull one thread. Who actually paid for that ad?"

## Scene 2 — Stakes / context
**VO:** "This is the race for one Texas seat in the U.S. Senate. Right now it is the most expensive Senate race in the country. The strange part is the money. Most of it does not come from the people running. It comes from outside groups called super PACs. In other words, a super PAC raises huge checks and buys ads. It just can not hand that money to a campaign. So the names on the ads are not the candidates. They are the groups. And every group picked a name that sounds like you. So stay with me, and let's pull the thread. Who is really behind the friendly name?"

## Scene 3 — The name game (Receipt 1, the big reveal)
**VO:** "Start with the biggest one. Texans for a Conservative Majority. The name sounds like a room full of neighbors. So we pulled its filing. Every super PAC must tell the government who pays it. That filing is public. It is free to read. And here is the part most people miss. The crowd is not a crowd. It is mostly one man. His name is John Nau. His family business distributes beer across Texas, and he is a billionaire. He put in about fifteen point six million dollars. A Wall Street boss named Stephen Schwarzman added four million. So a name that says Texans is really a few rich men. That group has spent twenty-four million dollars. All of it against one Republican, Ken Paxton."

## Scene 4 — The pattern (Receipt 2)
**VO:** "Then you pull the next thread. And it happens again. A group called Truth and Courage has spent about twenty-two million dollars. All of it against the Democrat, Colin Allred. The biggest name we can see in its filing is an out-of-state billionaire, Jeff Yass, at two million dollars. Same trick. A friendly name on the outside. Big checks on the inside."

## Scene 5 — Mid turn: not just one party (Receipt 3)
**VO:** "Now you might be thinking only one side does this. So did we. We were wrong. Look at a group called Lone Star Rising. It sounds like proud Texas folks. But the money is liberal money. One of its backers is a tech billionaire in California named Reid Hoffman, who put in a million and a half dollars. And it spends against a Democrat, Jasmine Crockett, inside the Democrats own race. So both sides wear the costume. That is the real story."

## Scene 6 — Why it matters (voter pivot, humanized)
**VO:** "Stop and think about who this is for. Ken Paxton and Colin Allred want to speak for thirty million Texans. Picture the woman who drives across town after work to vote. She gets one ballot. A billionaire in another state gets fifteen million dollars of airtime. That is the gap. And the loudest voices in this race are not a crowd of Texans. They are a handful of billionaires, some from out of state, writing million-dollar checks. They are spending money you were never meant to trace."

## Scene 7 — The hidden ones (honest gap)
**VO:** "And some threads still do not pull all the way. One group, WinSenate, spent ten million dollars to help Allred. But its filing does not name its donors yet. Another group spent almost five million against Wesley Hunt. All of it came through one out-of-state pass-through. So here is the honest thing. We do not know those names yet. The filing does not show them. And we will not make a name up."

## Scene 8 — Verdict (the costume turn)
**VO:** "So here is the punchline. The friendly names are a costume. Texans for a Conservative Majority is one beer billionaire. Truth and Courage hides its biggest checks. The warm name is the disguise. It is not the message. And the real face is written in only one place. The public filing."
