## 1. Central tension

When a Delaware shell that's three months old wires half a million dollars through a PAC branded "elect scientists" to back a pediatric surgeon, the money usually wins — except this time the voters watched the wire transfer happen in real time, and the receipt lost by fifteen points.

## 2. Transformation arc

Viewer ends realizing the channel has now shown them the same A.I.P.A.C.-aligned playbook three times in a row — Bush lost to $8M, Massie lost to $15.8M, Rabb survived $500K — and the only variable that changed wasn't the message, the candidate, or the district. It was whether the money stayed invisible.

## 3. Key reveal

The moment the Kimbark Foundation's Delaware registration date — December 19, 2025 — is placed next to its only two known disbursements ($500K to 314 Action Fund, $500K to EDW Action Fund). A foundation that existed for ninety-one days, gave away a million dollars, and shows up nowhere else on the public record. The viewer doesn't learn a new fact; they re-perceive "314 Action Fund spent $2.6M on Stanford" as "A.I.P.A.C.-aligned money spent $2.6M on Stanford wearing a lab coat."

## 4. Emotional stakes

If you don't watch, the next time a science-branded super PAC drops $3M in your primary, you'll read the candidate's bio and think the donors picked a doctor because they like doctors. If you do watch, you'll know to check the donor's incorporation date before you check the candidate's résumé — and you'll recognize the Kimbark template the next time a three-month-old foundation shows up in a House race that has nothing to do with Israel on its face.
