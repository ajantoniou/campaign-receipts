Thirty-five million dollars in one House primary. The most expensive primary for a single seat in American history. Thomas Massie is out, and the receipts are public.

Kentucky's Fourth district sits across the river from Cincinnati. One seat in the U.S. House. Thomas Massie has held it since twenty twelve. He is a libertarian Republican — an engineer with three patents, the kind of congressman who votes no on bills both parties want. He voted no on the Trump tax and immigration package called the big beautiful bill. He voted no on foreign-aid packages including Israel funding. He forced votes to release the Jeffrey Epstein files when his own leadership wanted them buried.

So Donald Trump endorsed someone else. The challenger was Ed Gallrein — a former Navy SEAL, new to politics, full slate of party-establishment backing. Trump posted on his platform that Massie was, quote, a totally ineffective loser. The Secretary of War flew into the district the weekend before primary day to campaign against him. Massie said on camera that no one could remember a sitting cabinet member campaigning in-district against a sitting congressman before.

Here is the punchline we are building toward. By election night, outside committees had spent about sixteen million dollars against Massie. Total race spend crossed thirty-five million. And the man who flipped the seat said his job now was to advance the president's agenda. So this was not a policy argument inside Kentucky. This was Washington reaching into one district to remove the one Republican who would not vote with the team on the issues that mattered most to specific donors.

Now the receipts. Three groups dominate the outside money. United Democracy Project — the super-PAC tied to the American Israel Public Affairs Committee, the lobby people pronounce AY-pack. The Republican Jewish Coalition — a GOP-aligned committee with the same foreign-policy focus. And Preserve America PAC — funded by Miriam Adelson, the casino heiress whose cycle commitment is reported around two hundred fifty million dollars across federal races. Their FEC filings, called Schedule E independent expenditures, are public. You can look up each committee yourself and read the line: oppose Massie, with dates and dollar amounts.

Days before the primary, Thomas Massie sat down with CBS News and put a number on it. Quote: ninety-five percent of my opponent's money comes from the Israeli lobby. That was Massie himself, on camera, naming the lobby, the donors, and the dollar share. He said this turned what would have been a sixty-forty race into a fifty-fifty race — because of the spending from AIPAC, the Republican Jewish Coalition, and Miriam Adelson.

Trump on Truth Social. Hegseth on the ground. AIPAC, RJC, and Preserve America on the airwaves. Same week, same district, same theme: Massie is not on the team.

Election night, Kentucky. Gallrein took roughly fifty-four percent. Massie ended around forty-five. About a ten-thousand-vote margin in a district he had won by seventy and eighty percent in past primaries. By Kentucky law, the call was made earlier than any KY-4 primary in modern memory.

When Gallrein walked on stage, here is what he said his job would be. Quote: my focus is on advancing the president's and the party's agenda, to put America first and Kentucky always. The man who took out the most independent Republican in the House framed his win as a return to the team.

So here is what that means in plain English. Thirty-five million dollars did not come to Kentucky to argue about Kentucky. It came to remove one no vote in Washington — on Israel funding, on foreign aid, on a bill the donors wanted passed. The voters who showed up still made the choice. But the choices on the ballot were shaped, weeks earlier, by who could afford to define each candidate first. That is the receipt for the most expensive House primary in American history.
