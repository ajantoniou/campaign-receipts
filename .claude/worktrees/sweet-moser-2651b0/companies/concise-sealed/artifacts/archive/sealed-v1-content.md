## Foreword (2026 frame — draft)

Millions of Americans voted for a *set of promises* — a vision described in plain words before lobbying pressure, legislative compromise, and geopolitical reality reshaped what was feasible.

This edition does not ask you to cheer or condemn. It asks you to **remember the “before” clearly enough** that the “after” can be judged without mediation.

Sources are cited so you can verify. Interpretation is yours.

## Part I — The campaign record (2015–2016)

### Chapter 1 — Trail mechanics

This chapter shows you how to read the rest of the book. It uses a few real campaign moments as training wheels. The words are short on purpose. The ideas are not.

#### Section A — Format of entries (how every page in this book works)

Each entry in SEALED uses the same three-part shape. No surprises. You can flip to any chapter and know what to expect.

1. **The margin rail (the “where and when” box).**  
   We list the time, the place, the kind of event, and the kind of crowd when we know them. That is not decoration. It is so you can picture the room.

2. **The verbatim block (the promise, in their words).**  
   The quote is in *italics*. It is what was actually said, from a primary transcript. We do not paraphrase the promise in the block.

3. **The body (what came after, in the public record).**  
   The next paragraphs are the main meal. They point to **official paper** you can look up: laws, orders, votes, agency filings, data tables. We do not tell you the “right” answer. We show you the trail.

**At a glance — the three-part shape**

```
[ MARGIN RAIL: time | place | setting | audience ]
[ VERBATIM QUOTE — from a live transcript or official text ]
[ BODY — plain-English walk through the paper trail; you pick the grade ]
```

#### Section B — Margin rails decoded (why the left column matters)

A “rail” is just a label for the context of a promise. Same word, different room, different pressure.

- **Launch rail** — the first time the line is said to a big live audience (for example, the 2015 Trump Tower speech on TV).  
- **Interview rail** — the same idea, but in a follow-up Q&A the same day or week. Tone can shift when the cameras move closer.  
- **Rally rail** — later in the campaign or after the election, often with a fired-up crowd (“thank you” tours count here).

Why repeat rails across entries? So you can **compare apples to apples**. If the promise sounds the same in a tower, in a green room, and in an arena, that tells you something about how sticky the language was.

#### Section C — Grade key & reader assignment (your scorecard, not ours)

This book never prints a final verdict like “promise broken” in our voice. You fill that in.

Use only five labels:

| Label | Plain meaning |
|-------|----------------|
| **KEPT** | They followed through in a way that matches the words. |
| **PARTIAL** | Real movement, but not the full promise. |
| **BROKEN** | Clear retreat or long stall where action was possible. |
| **BLOCKED** | Courts, Congress, or hard facts stopped cold progress. |
| **MOOT** | The world changed so much that the promise no longer fits. |

How do you pick one? Read the quote. Read the paper trail we point to. Then pick the label that fits **your** reading of the evidence.

### Chapter 3 — Trade & reciprocity

The anti-establishment frame in Chapters 1 and 2 had an economic arm: trade as the mechanism by which other countries took American jobs and wealth. This chapter fixes the trade rails from the campaign — NAFTA, TPP, China currency manipulation — so readers can pair them to the policy instruments that followed. The language was specific, the papers are public, and the comparison is yours.

#### Section A — NAFTA / TPP pivot

##### Entry T1 — “Worst trade deal” / NAFTA / TPP

**Rail — September 26, 2016 · Hofstra University, NY · first general election debate (Commission on Presidential Debates).**  
Primary transcript: https://www.debates.org/voter-education/debate-transcripts/september-26-2016-debate-transcript/

*“Your husband signed NAFTA, which was one of the worst things that ever happened to the manufacturing industry.”*

*“NAFTA is the worst trade deal maybe ever signed anywhere, but certainly ever signed in this country.”*

*“And now you want to approve Trans-Pacific Partnership. You were totally in favor of it. Then you heard what I was saying, how bad it is, and you said, I can’t win that debate.”*

**Paper trail:** USTR press releases; Federal Register tariff notices; USMCA legal text in USTR archives.

**Your grade:** Compare the "worst trade deal" language to USMCA agreement text and TPP withdrawal notices — did the instruments match the campaign characterization?

#### Section C — “Fair trade, not free trade”

##### Entry T3 — Stop jobs leaving / renegotiate deals

**Rail — September 26, 2016 · Hofstra · jobs question continuation.**

## Part II — Alliances & force posture

### Chapter 6 — NATO & burden-sharing

The 2016 campaign was explicit about NATO: allied countries were rich, the United States was bearing a disproportionate cost, and the arrangement needed renegotiation. "Questioning NATO" was framed as leverage, not disengagement. These entries freeze that language so readers can compare it to the diplomatic record that followed.

#### Section A — Alliance cost-sharing

#### Case study 4 — NATO & burden-sharing

**Promise (verbatim excerpt)**  
"Why aren’t they paying? Because they weren’t paying. Since I did this — this was a year ago — all of a sudden, they’re paying. And I’ve been given a lot — a lot of credit for it." — CPD third presidential debate, October 19, 2016.

**What the archive captures**  
A **cost-sharing** demand framed as leverage already producing results before Inauguration Day. The phrase "fan of NATO" sits in the same answer, making the pledge dual: reform contributions, not abandon the alliance.

**What to verify**  
NATO published defence-expenditure summaries (annual, by member state); U.S. DoD posture statements for Europe; congressional NATO-related hearings from 2017 onward.

**What this reveals**  
The promise bundled two distinct claims: (a) allies were underpaying, and (b) Trump’s public challenge was already correcting that. (a) had prior NATO documentation; (b) requires readers to weigh causation vs. coincidence against the published timeline.
