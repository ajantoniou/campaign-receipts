- Choose: warm-anonymous, slightly mysterious, neighborhood-friendly

## Voice fingerprint

- **Anonymous-conspiratorial-friendly** — like a friendly stranger at
  a bar, not a creepy lurker
- **Neighborhood-grounded** — references to actual places, streets,
  vibes
- **Slightly playful** — anonymity allows wit
- **Never crude, never edgy-for-edgy's-sake** — anonymity is the
  feature; vulgarity is not
- **Reassuring on safety** — "verified posters" framing, "we know
  who's saying what behind the scenes"
- **Anti-Nextdoor** without being directly hostile

## Visual aesthetic

- **Clean modern** with anonymity cues (silhouettes, generic avatars,
  no real photos of people)
- **Map-grounded** — geographic visuals, neighborhood maps, distance
  indicators
- **Charlotte-specific** — incorporate local landmarks subtly
- **Color palette:** desaturated city tones (slate, rust, brick) with
  one bright accent (electric green or amber for "live" indicators)
- **Typography:** modern sans-serif (Söhne, Inter, IBM Plex)
- **AVOID:** stock photos of people, "community" clichés, social
  app cliches (chat bubbles + gradients)

## Brand-product connection

The verification model is the brand. Marketing copy reinforces:
- "Anonymous to your neighbors. Accountable to us."
- "Verified posters only. No bots, no fakes."
- "What you'd say if no one knew it was you. Without the trolls."

## Banned visuals/copy

### Visual
- Stock photos of "diverse smiling people on phone"
- Cartoon dating-app aesthetic
- Generic "social app" gradient backgrounds (purple to blue)
- Heart icons, chat bubbles with gradients
- Map pins that look like generic Maps
- Anything sexualized

### Copy
- "Connect with your neighbors!" (Nextdoor cliché)
- "Find your community" (over-used, meaningless)
- "Like Twitter for [thing]" (lazy, dismissive)
- "Anonymous and free" without qualification (legal risk)
- "Talk to anyone nearby" (sounds creepy)
- Any implication this is a dating / hookup app
