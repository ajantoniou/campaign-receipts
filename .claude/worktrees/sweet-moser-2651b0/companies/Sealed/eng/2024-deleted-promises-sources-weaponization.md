## CHAPTER 4 REPLACEMENT — ICE detentions

Mahmoud Khalil was the first big name. He is a Columbia graduate student. He helped lead the Gaza protests on campus. On March 8, 2025, plainclothes ICE agents took him from his apartment in New York. He was a lawful permanent resident. The agents had no warrant. He was flown to a detention center in Louisiana, a thousand miles from his school and his pregnant wife. He was held for 104 days. A federal judge ordered him released on bail on June 20, 2025. His case is still in court. ([NPR, June 20 2025](https://www.npr.org/2025/06/20/nx-s1-5440351/judge-orders-release-of-columbia-activist-mahmoud-khalil); [Center for Constitutional Rights court filing](https://ccrjustice.org/home/press-center/press-releases/court-rules-mahmoud-khalil-s-lawsuit-challenging-his-unlawful))

Rumeysa Ozturk was next. She is a Ph.D. student at Tufts. On March 25, 2025, plainclothes ICE agents took her off the sidewalk in Somerville, Massachusetts. The reason on the record was an op-ed she co-wrote in the Tufts student paper. The op-ed asked the university to listen to its student government on Gaza. She was moved to a detention facility in Louisiana. A federal judge in Vermont ordered her released six weeks later, on May 9, 2025. An immigration judge later ended her removal case entirely. ([ACLU Massachusetts](https://www.aclum.org/press-releases/immigration-judge-terminates-removal-proceedings-against-child-development-scholar-rumeysa-ozturk/); [Boston Globe, July 17 2025](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2025/07/17/metro/rumeysa-ozturk-describes-ice-detention-op-ed/))

Mohsen Mahdawi was a Columbia student too. He helped organize the same protests as Khalil. He had a green card. On April 14, 2025, he went to his U.S. citizenship interview in Vermont. ICE was waiting in the lobby. They took him into custody at the interview. On April 30, 2025, a federal judge in Vermont ordered him released on bail. The judge wrote that Mahdawi had "made substantial claims that his detention is the result of retaliation for protected speech." His immigration case is still open. ([ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/federal-court-orders-columbia-student-mohsen-mahdawi-released-on-bail); [CNN, April 30 2025](https://www.cnn.com/2025/04/30/us/vermont-judge-orders-release-of-mohsen-mahdawi))

Badar Khan Suri was a Georgetown postdoctoral fellow. He taught a class on South Asian minority rights. He is an Indian national on a valid visa. In March 2025, masked federal officers took him from outside his home in Rosslyn, Virginia. The government revoked his visa. He was flown to a detention facility in Texas. He was held there for about two months. On May 14, 2025, a federal judge in Virginia ordered him released. The judge wrote that the detention violated the First Amendment and the Fifth Amendment. ([Washington Post, May 14 2025](https://www.washingtonpost.com/immigration/2025/05/14/georgetown-researcher-suri-virginia-texas/); [NPR, May 14 2025](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/14/g-s1-66606/georgetown-badar-khan-suri-immigration-release))

These are four cases. There are more. They share a pattern. The person was a foreign student or scholar at a major U.S. university. The person had spoken at a protest or written an opinion piece about Gaza. The arrest was sudden and far from home. The detention was long and far from family. The release came only when a federal judge looked at the file and used the words *First Amendment* in the order.

The page said the government would not do this. The first months of the term did this.

## CHAPTER 4 REPLACEMENT — home visits over social media

This one is harder to source. The pattern is real. The named cases are fewer. Here is what is on the record.

In April 2025, the FBI and other agencies raided the homes of pro-Palestine student organizers in Ann Arbor, Ypsilanti, and Canton Township, Michigan. Agents seized phones and laptops. Four people were detained that day and released. The official reason given was a vandalism investigation. The Michigan Attorney General later confirmed she had asked the FBI to step in. Critics noted that property-damage cases of this size are not usually a federal matter. ([Inside Higher Ed, April 23 2025](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/students/free-speech/2025/04/23/union-police-raids-targeted-michigan-palestine-activists); [The Intercept, June 15 2025](https://theintercept.com/2025/06/15/university-michigan-fbi-raid-students-palestine/))

In January 2026, two Miami Beach detectives knocked on the door of Raquel Pacheco. She is a U.S. citizen, an Army National Guard veteran, and a former candidate for state office. Days earlier, she had posted a Facebook comment criticizing the city's mayor over his stance on Israel. The officers held up a phone with a screenshot of her post and asked her, on her doorstep, if she had written it. She filmed the encounter. Internal emails later showed the mayor had flagged the post to the police chief himself. The American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee filed a federal lawsuit on her behalf. ([The Intercept, January 20 2026](https://theintercept.com/2026/01/20/miami-beach-mayor-meiner-police-speech-israel/); [Axios Miami, January 30 2026](https://www.axios.com/local/miami/2026/01/30/miami-beach-mayor-facebook-post-police); [ADC lawsuit announcement](https://adc.org/adc-miami-beach-lawsuit/))

The Council on American-Islamic Relations says it has logged a much larger pattern. Since the war began, CAIR reports it has received many calls from Palestinian, Arab, and Muslim Americans who say federal agents came to the door to ask about social media posts. CAIR has put out formal statements calling the visits illegal. The full count of door-knocks is not on the public record. The pattern of the calls is. ([CAIR press release](https://www.cair.com/press_releases/cair-says-fbi-illegally-interrogating-people-about-social-media-posts-critical-of-israel/); [Washington Post, March 31 2024](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2024/03/31/fbi-oklahoma-social-media-gaza/))

That is what is documented. Two named incidents on U.S. citizens. One civil-liberties organization with a public log. A federal lawsuit pending. The page promised this would end. It has not ended.
