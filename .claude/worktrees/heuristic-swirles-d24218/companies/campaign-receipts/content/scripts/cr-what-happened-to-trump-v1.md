## BEAT 2 — He said it again in 2024 — in writing (0:30–2:00)

You remember the voice. Nine years ago, he said the wars were a scam. A lot of people voted for him because of that. You may have been one of them.

Then 2024 came around. And he said it again. This time, in writing.

On his campaign website. Under a section called "Renew American Strength."

[On screen: verbatim block, audit-document type, with deleted_after stamp pending.]

The page said this: "President Trump will defend America against all threats, protect America against all dangers, and keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars."

Read that line again. **Keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars.**

There was another page on the site. It was called the 20 Core Promises. Number 8 on the list said this: "Prevent world war three. Restore peace in Europe and in the Middle East."

That's two pages. Two promises. Same cycle. Same brand. The deal was the same as in 2015.

You read the page. You voted on the page. So did a lot of other people.

I want you to hold that page in your head, because in a minute the page is going to be gone.

## BEAT 3 — On January 20, 2025, the page came down (2:00–3:30)

He was sworn in on January 20, 2025.

That same day, donaldjtrump.com changed.

Not edited. Not moved to a sub-page. Not archived in a "past statements" folder.

Gone.

The policy section came down. The 20 Core Promises page came down. The "keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars" line came down.

What was left on the site was a donation form.

If you go to donaldjtrump.com today and look for the words you voted on, you will not find them. The campaign site does not remember.

There is one place those words still live. It's free. It's called the Wayback Machine. It's a project that takes snapshots of websites so the internet has a memory the websites themselves don't.

The Wayback Machine remembers the page. The campaign site does not.

That's the first thing I want you to hold. The deal you voted on is now only readable in an archive.

The second thing is what happened five months later.

## BEAT 4 — Five months in, the planes flew (3:30–5:30)

June 21, 2025.

Seven B-2 stealth bombers took off from a base in Missouri. They flew to Iran. They dropped fourteen bunker-busters on three sites. The sites were called Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan. A U.S. submarine fired Tomahawk missiles at the same time.

Start to finish, about 25 minutes.

The operation had a name. The Pentagon called it Operation Midnight Hammer.

At about ten p.m. that night, the President spoke from the White House. He called the strike a "spectacular military success." He said the sites had been "completely and totally obliterated."

The Secretary of Defense spoke the next morning. He called the strike "very narrowly tailored." He called it "necessary."

I want you to hold that word. **Necessary.**

The campaign page used the same word, nine months earlier, in the opposite direction. The page said keep America out of **unnecessary** foreign wars.

So one word does a lot of work in this story. The page said the bar was "unnecessary." The strike said the bar was "necessary." And the person who decided where the bar was — was the same person who wrote the page.

You voted on the page. The man holding the pen was the man who took the page down.

That's the gap. Not a lie. Not a flip. A gap. The page set the rule, and then the same hand decided the rule didn't apply.

If you're thinking right now, *okay, maybe this was a one-off — maybe the strike was the exception* — hold that thought. Because the audit has been done. And it's been done for nine years.

## BEAT 5 — The 2016 audit already graded this (5:30–7:30)

There's a book called SEALED 2016. It's a 145-promise audit of the first Trump term. Same rubric for every promise. The math is on the cover: 46 KEPT, 51 PARTIAL, 40 BROKEN, 8 reader-decides.

Two promises in that book matter for what you just saw.

Promise number 46. "Tear up the Iran nuclear deal." Verdict: **KEPT.**

In May 2018, the President signed a memorandum pulling the United States out of the deal. The deal had a name. It was called the J.C.P.O.A. The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. The whole job of that deal was to make a war with Iran unnecessary. He promised to tear it up. He tore it up. KEPT.

Promise number 51. "No more endless wars." Verdict: **PARTIAL.**

So here's where the audit gets quiet and the receipt gets loud.

The kept promise dismantled the deal whose entire job was to make 2025's strike unnecessary. The partial promise was the one that said the strike shouldn't have happened in the first place.

Both verdicts are in the same book. Same rubric. Same page. The book doesn't take a side. The book just grades.

That's the punchline. The contradiction is not between him and his critics. The contradiction is inside his own record. KEPT on tearing up the off-ramp. PARTIAL on the wars that off-ramp was built to prevent.

If you read only one of those promises, you get a hero. If you read the other one too, you get a man who tore down the fence and then was surprised the cows got out.

You don't have to flip your vote to see that. You only have to read both pages.

## BEAT 6 — The weaponization promise broke the same way (7:30–10:00)

The Iran page wasn't the only page on the site that came down on January 20.

Number 9 on the 20 Core Promises said this: "End the weaponization of government against the american people."

Plain English: stop using the government to punish people for speaking up.

A lot of people voted for that line. It was one of the biggest lines on the site.

Here's what the first few months looked like.

March 8, 2025. Mahmoud Khalil. A graduate student at Columbia. A lawful permanent resident — a green-card holder. Federal agents took him from his apartment in New York. No warrant. They flew him to a detention center in Louisiana. He sat there for 104 days.

A federal judge ordered him released on June 20. The judge cited the First Amendment.

March 25, 2025. Rumeysa Ozturk. A Ph.D. student at Tufts. Federal agents took her off a sidewalk in Somerville, Massachusetts. The reason given was an op-ed she had co-written.

A federal judge in Vermont ordered her released on May 9. An immigration judge later ended the case against her.

April 14, 2025. Mohsen Mahdawi. A Columbia student with a green card. Federal agents took him at his U.S. citizenship interview. The interview itself. In Vermont.

A federal judge ordered him released two weeks later. The judge wrote that the detention raised "substantial claims" of "retaliation for protected speech."

March 2025. Badar Khan Suri. A postdoctoral researcher at Georgetown, on a valid visa. Federal agents took him from outside his home in Virginia. They moved him to Texas. He sat there about two months.

A federal judge in Virginia ordered him released on May 14. The order named the First Amendment and the Fifth Amendment.

Four people. Four cases. Four federal judges. Four times a judge had to write the words First Amendment in an order before the door opened.

The page said end the weaponization. The first months ran the weaponization on speech.

This is what I mean by a pattern. One Iran strike could be a one-off. Four detentions, four judges, four First Amendment orders — that's a page that promised one thing and an action that did the other thing. Same shape as the Iran page. Different subject. Same shape.

## BEAT 7 — You voted on a page. The page is gone. (10:00–12:00)

[On screen: the donaldjtrump.com 2024 capture of the "keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars" line. Hold 4 seconds in silence. Then overlay, audit-document type:
**PAGE DELETED — JAN 20, 2025**
Cut to black.]

So here is where we are.

You voted on a page. The page is gone. The verdict is yours.

Nobody is telling you the vote was wrong. Nobody is telling you to flip. The page was the deal you were offered. You read it. You voted on it. That part is on the record, and it was done in good faith.

What is also on the record: the page came down on day one, the planes flew in month five, the four students sat in detention, and the audit of his first term already graded the kept promise that took the off-ramp away.

You're allowed to hold both of those things at once. You voted in good faith, AND the page is gone. Those are not in tension with each other. The first one is about you. The second one is about him.

There's one small thing you can do before the next election. It costs nothing. It takes five seconds.

Before you vote, go to the candidate's website. Find the page you'd be voting on. Save it. Screenshot it. Send it to yourself. The Wayback Machine is free, and it remembers — but you don't need the Wayback Machine if you saved the page yourself.

The campaign site is not built to remember. You are.

Next time, save the page before you vote.

Be kind.

[5–8s music-bed-only tail. No verdict stamp on screen. Black. Then fade.]
