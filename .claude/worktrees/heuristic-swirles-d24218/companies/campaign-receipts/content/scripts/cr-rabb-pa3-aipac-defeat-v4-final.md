## VO script

## Scene 1 — Hook

A pediatric surgeon who ran the Black Doctors COVID Consortium just came in third in a Philadelphia primary the political establishment was supposed to win. The state rep who beat her, most of the country has never heard of. And the most interesting thing in this race isn't either of them — it's a foundation in Delaware that was ninety-one days old when it wired five hundred thousand dollars to the P.A.C. that backed the surgeon.

## Scene 2 — The race

On May 19th, Pennsylvania certified the Third Congressional District primary. Chris Rabb, a state rep from Mount Airy, took forty-four percent. State Senator Sharif Street took thirty. Dr. Ala Stanford — the doctor who set up free COVID testing tents in Black neighborhoods when nobody else would — took twenty-four. Longtime Congressman Dwight Evans is retiring, so this is an open seat. Whoever wins the Democratic primary wins the seat. This was the race. And the money knew it.

## Scene 3 — The shell

Picture a single P.O. box in Wilmington, Delaware. No staff. No website. Just a name on a piece of paper. On December 19th, 2025, a group called the Kimbark Foundation registered as a nonprofit at that kind of address. Kimbark's very first gift, sixty-five days in, went to a P.A.C. called EDW Action Fund — five hundred thousand dollars. Then on day ninety-one, the second gift: five hundred thousand to something called the 314 Action Fund — a federal P.A.C. In other words, an outside political group that can raise and spend unlimited money to influence a race. Two gifts. One million dollars. Then nothing. You can pull 314 Action Fund's filings yourself — F.E.C. committee C00633248.

## Scene 4 — The lab coat

The 314 Action Fund's pitch is a good one on paper: a P.A.C. built to put more scientists in Congress. And once that five hundred thousand from Kimbark landed, 314 Action Fund spent three and a half million dollars on independent expenditures backing Dr. Stanford — the only scientist in the field. In other words, ad buys and mailers paid for directly by the P.A.C., never touching Stanford's campaign account. The F.E.C. breakdown: two-point-seven-five million on advertising, six hundred fifty-six thousand on direct mail, plus media production and text messages. Reporting from Drop Site News and Common Dreams traces the Kimbark shell back to donors aligned with A.I.P.A.C. — a major Israel-policy lobby in Washington. A.I.P.A.C. denies any connection. The receipt does not.

## Scene 5 — The pattern

Here's the punchline you only see if you've been watching. This is the third time this channel has shown you the same playbook. In 2024, Cori Bush — the St. Louis nurse and pastor who fed protesters during Ferguson — lost her Missouri primary after A.I.P.A.C.'s super-P.A.C., U.D.P., spent eight and a half million dollars against her. Last week, Kentucky Congressman Thomas Massie lost his primary after pro-Israel groups dropped fifteen-point-eight million — U.D.P., the R.J.C. Victory Fund, and an Adelson-linked transfer. Massie said it on camera: "ninety-five percent of my opponent's money came from the Israeli lobby." Bush, eight-point-five million, lost. Massie, fifteen-point-eight million, lost. But Rabb — three-point-five million spent against him — won by fifteen points. Same playbook. Three outcomes. One variable.

## Scene 6 — What changed

So what changed in Philly? It came down to two days. On April 24th, A.O.C. endorsed Rabb — which put him on a national small-dollar fundraising list overnight. On April 30th, Hasan Piker showed up at Malcolm X Park in West Philly and canvassed for Rabb on a live Twitch stream. The money didn't stay invisible long enough to work.

## Scene 7 — Verdict

Common Dreams ran the headline "A.I.P.A.C. Lost!" the day the result was certified. The verdict here is narrower and harder. A.I.P.A.C. didn't lose. The shell lost. The next shell will have a different name, a different state of incorporation, and a different month on the registration. The playbook is still on the shelf. F.E.C. committee C00633248 is the whole episode.

## Scene 8 — CTA

Full receipt at campaignreceipts dot com slash race slash P-A dash three. Sources in the description. Here's what this race taught us: when a ninety-one-day-old foundation turns up in a primary, the incorporation date tells you more than the candidate's résumé does.
