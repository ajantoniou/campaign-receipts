## BEAT 2 — He said it again in 2024 — in writing (0:30–1:45)

You remember the voice. Nine years ago, he said the wars were a scam. A lot of people voted for him because of that. You may have been one of them.

Then 2024 came. He said it again. This time in writing.

It was on his campaign website. Under a section called "Renew American Strength."

[On screen: verbatim block, audit-document type, with deleted_after stamp pending.]

The page said this: "President Trump will defend America against all threats, protect America against all dangers, and keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars."

Read it again. **Keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars.**

There was a second page. It was called the 20 Core Promises. Number 8 said this: "Prevent world war three. Restore peace in Europe and in the Middle East."

Two pages. Two promises. Same cycle. Same deal as 2015.

You read the page. You voted on the page. So did a lot of other people.

Hold that page in your head. Picture where it sits. The website. The headline. The line about wars.

In a minute, it won't be there.

## BEAT 3 — On January 20, 2025, the page came down (1:45–3:00)

He was sworn in on January 20, 2025.

That same day, donaldjtrump.com changed.

Not edited. Not moved. Not put in an old-statements folder.

Gone.

The policy page came down. The 20 Core Promises came down. The "unnecessary foreign wars" line came down.

What was left was a donation form.

Go to donaldjtrump.com today. Look for the words you voted on. You will not find them. The campaign site does not remember.

There is one place those words still live. It's free. It's called the Wayback Machine. It takes snapshots of websites. The internet has a memory the websites themselves don't.

The Wayback Machine remembers the page. The campaign site does not.

That's the first thing I want you to hold. The deal you voted on now only lives in an archive.

The second thing is what happened five months later.

Hold the date in your head. January 20. The page came down.

Count five months forward.

## BEAT 4 — Five months in, the planes flew (3:00–4:30)

June 21, 2025.

Seven B-2 stealth bombers took off from a base in Missouri. They flew to Iran. They dropped fourteen bunker-busters on three sites. The sites were Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan. A U.S. submarine fired Tomahawk missiles at the same time.

Start to finish, about 25 minutes.

The operation had a name. The Pentagon called it Operation Midnight Hammer.

Around ten p.m. that night, the President spoke from the White House. He called the strike a "spectacular military success." He said the sites had been "completely and totally obliterated."

The Secretary of Defense spoke the next morning. He called the strike "very narrowly tailored." He called it "necessary."

Hold that word. **Necessary.**

The campaign page used the same word nine months earlier. In the opposite direction. The page said keep America out of **unnecessary** foreign wars.

So one word does a lot of work here. The page said the bar was "unnecessary." The strike said the bar was "necessary." The person who decided where the bar was — was the same person who wrote the page.

You voted on the page. The man holding the pen was the man who took the page down.

That's the gap. Not a lie. Not a flip. A gap. The page set the rule. Then the same hand decided the rule didn't apply.

So is this a one-off? One bad week. One hard call.

That's the fair question to ask. The 2016 audit already graded this one. The grade is going to surprise you.

## BEAT 5 — The 2016 audit already graded this (4:30–6:30)

There's a book called SEALED 2016. It's an audit of his first term. One hundred forty-five promises. Same rubric for each one. The math is on the cover. 46 KEPT. 51 PARTIAL. 40 BROKEN. 8 reader-decides.

Two promises in that book matter for what you just saw.

Promise number 46. "Tear up the Iran nuclear deal." Verdict: **KEPT.**

In May 2018, the President signed a memo. He pulled the United States out of the deal. The deal had a name. It was called the J.C.P.O.A. The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. The whole job of that deal was to make a war with Iran unnecessary. He promised to tear it up. He tore it up. KEPT.

Promise number 51. "No more endless wars." Verdict: **PARTIAL.**

Now stay with me. These two grades belong in the same sentence.

The kept promise tore down the deal whose whole job was to make 2025's strike unnecessary. The partial promise was the one that said the strike shouldn't have happened at all.

Both verdicts are in the same book. Same rubric. Same page. The book doesn't take a side. The book just grades.

That's the punchline. The contradiction is not between him and his critics. The contradiction is inside his own record. KEPT on tearing down the off-ramp. PARTIAL on the wars that off-ramp was built to stop.

Read one of those promises. You get a hero. Read the other one too. You get a man who tore down the fence and then was surprised the cows got out.

You don't have to flip your vote to see that. You only have to read both pages.

So that's the Iran page. One page. One strike. Graded.

One Iran strike could be a one-off. Count the judges.

## BEAT 6 — The same shape, on a second page (6:30–9:30)

Number 9 on the 20 Core Promises said this: "End the weaponization of government against the american people."

In plain English: stop using the government to punish people for speaking up.

A lot of people voted for that line. It was one of the biggest lines on the site.

Here's what the first few months looked like.

March 8, 2025. Mahmoud Khalil. A graduate student at Columbia. A green-card holder. Federal agents took him from his apartment in New York. No warrant. They flew him to a jail in Louisiana. He sat there for 104 days.

A federal judge ordered him released on June 20. The judge named the First Amendment.

March 25, 2025. Rumeysa Ozturk. A Ph.D. student at Tufts. Federal agents took her off a sidewalk in Somerville, Massachusetts. The reason given was an op-ed she had co-written.

A federal judge in Vermont ordered her released on May 9. An immigration judge later ended the case.

Stay with me. Two more.

April 14, 2025. Mohsen Mahdawi. A Columbia student with a green card. Federal agents took him at his U.S. citizenship interview. The interview itself. In Vermont.

A federal judge ordered him released two weeks later. The judge wrote that the detention raised "substantial claims" of "retaliation for protected speech."

March 2025. Badar Khan Suri. A postdoc researcher at Georgetown. A valid visa. Federal agents took him from outside his home in Virginia. They moved him to Texas. He sat there about two months.

A federal judge in Virginia ordered him released on May 14. The order named the First Amendment and the Fifth Amendment.

Now count.

Four people. Four cases. Four federal judges. Four times a judge had to write the words "First Amendment" before the door opened.

The page said end it. The first months ran it.

This is what a pattern looks like. One Iran strike could be a one-off. Four detentions, four judges, four First Amendment orders — that's a page that promised one thing and an action that did the other thing.

Same shape as the Iran page. Different subject. Same shape.

## BEAT 7 — You voted on a page. The page is gone. (9:30–12:00)

[On screen: the donaldjtrump.com 2024 capture of the "keep America out of unnecessary foreign wars" line. Hold 4 seconds in silence. Then overlay, audit-document type:
**PAGE DELETED — JAN 20, 2025**
Cut to black.]

So here is where we are.

You voted on a page. The page is gone. The verdict is yours.

Nobody is telling you the vote was wrong. Nobody is telling you to flip. The page was the deal you were offered. You read it. You voted on it. That part is on the record. It was done in good faith.

What is also on the record. The page came down on day one. The planes flew in month five. The four students sat in detention. The 2016 audit already graded the kept promise that took the off-ramp away.

You're allowed to hold both of those at once. You voted in good faith, AND the page is gone. Those are not in tension. The first one is about you. The second one is about him.

There's one small thing you can do before the next election. It costs nothing. It takes five seconds.

Before you vote, go to the candidate's website. Find the page you'd be voting on. Save it. Screenshot it. Send it to yourself. The Wayback Machine is free, and it remembers. But you don't need the Wayback Machine if you saved the page yourself.

The campaign site is not built to remember. You are.

Next time, save the page before you vote.

Be kind.
