Three and a half million dollars. A Delaware shell that was barely three months old spent it trying to beat Pennsylvania state rep Chris Rabb. He won by fifteen points. Here is the receipt.

The candidate that shell was backing was Dr. Ala Stanford — a pediatric surgeon who ran the Black Doctors COVID Consortium, the group that set up free testing tents in Black Philadelphia neighborhoods when nobody else would. She came in third. The state rep who beat her, most of the country has never heard of. But the most interesting thing in this race isn't either of them — it's the foundation that wired that money.

On May 19th, Pennsylvania certified the Third Congressional District primary. Chris Rabb, a state rep from Mount Airy, took forty-four percent. State Senator Sharif Street took thirty. Dr. Stanford took twenty-four. Longtime Congressman Dwight Evans is retiring, so this is an open seat — whoever wins the Democratic primary wins the seat. This was the race, and the money knew it.

But first, look at the foundation. Picture a single P.O. box in Wilmington, Delaware. No staff. No website. Just a name on a piece of paper. On December 19th, 2025, a group called the Kimbark Foundation registered as a nonprofit at that kind of address. Kimbark's very first gift, about two months in, went to a P.A.C. called EDW Action Fund — five hundred thousand dollars. The second gift, about three months in: another five hundred thousand to something called the 314 Action Fund — a federal P.A.C. that backs candidates with science backgrounds. Two gifts. One million dollars. Then nothing. You can pull 314 Action Fund's filings yourself on the F.E.C. website — the Federal Election Commission, the public database that tracks every dollar in federal politics. Same committee, every wire, every dollar.

The 314 Action Fund's pitch is a good one on paper: a P.A.C. built to put more scientists in Congress. And once that five hundred thousand from Kimbark landed, 314 Action Fund spent three and a half million dollars on independent expenditures backing Dr. Stanford — the only scientist in the field. In other words, ad buys and mailers paid for directly by the P.A.C., never touching Stanford's campaign account. The F.E.C. breakdown: two-point-seven-five million on advertising, six hundred fifty-six thousand on direct mail, plus media production and text messages. Reporting from Drop Site News and Common Dreams traces the Kimbark shell back to donors aligned with A.I.P.A.C. — a major Israel-policy lobby in Washington. A.I.P.A.C. denies any connection. The receipt does not.

Here is the part you only see if you've been watching. This is the third time this channel has shown you the same playbook. In 2024, Cori Bush — the St. Louis nurse and pastor who fed protesters during Ferguson — lost her Missouri primary after A.I.P.A.C.'s super-P.A.C., U.D.P., spent eight and a half million dollars against her. Last week, Kentucky Congressman Thomas Massie lost his primary after pro-Israel groups dropped fifteen-point-eight million — U.D.P., the R.J.C. Victory Fund, and an Adelson-linked transfer. Bush, eight-point-five million, lost. Massie, fifteen-point-eight million, lost. But Rabb — three-point-five million spent against him — won by fifteen points. Same playbook. Three outcomes. One variable.

So what changed in Philly? It came down to two days. On April 24th, A.O.C. endorsed Rabb — which put him on a national small-dollar fundraising list overnight. On April 30th, Hasan Piker showed up at Malcolm X Park in West Philly and canvassed for Rabb on a live Twitch stream. The money didn't stay invisible long enough to work.

The verdict here is narrower and harder. A.I.P.A.C. did not lose. The shell lost. The next shell will have a different name, a different state of incorporation, and a different month on the registration. The playbook is still on the shelf. One F.E.C. committee filing is the whole episode.

Full receipt and every source linked below. Here's what this race taught us: when a brand-new foundation shows up in a primary, the incorporation date tells you more than the candidate's résumé does.
