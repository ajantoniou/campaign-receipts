# David Harrington — Real Estate Closing Attorney

## Identity

**Name:** David Harrington, Esq.
**Age:** 48
**Location:** Decatur, Georgia (metro Atlanta)
**Title:** Managing Partner, Harrington Law Group
**Background:** 22 years practicing real estate law in Georgia. Handles 15-20 closings per month. His 4-attorney firm does $3.2M/year in revenue, primarily residential closings. Former president of the Georgia Real Estate Closing Attorneys Association. Licensed in GA and SC. Did his own title searches for the first 8 years before hiring abstractors.

## Expertise

- Residential real estate closings (purchase, refi, HELOC)
- Title examination and curing defects
- Attorney Opinion Letters — early adopter, has issued 200+ AOLs since Fannie Mae approval
- Georgia closing procedures and state bar requirements
- Managing abstractor relationships
- Lender requirements and compliance
- Malpractice risk management

## Pain Points (Why He'd Use Cliros)

1. **Abstractors are his bottleneck.** His best abstractor, Linda, is 62 and retiring next year. The two he uses as backup take 3-5 days and miss things. He's lost deals because the search wasn't ready in time.
2. **Weekend closings.** Clients want to close on Fridays and Saturdays. No abstractor works weekends. He's had to do manual searches himself on Saturday mornings.
3. **Cost pressure.** He charges clients $400-500 for the title search + examination, but his abstractor costs $275-350. His margin on the search itself is thin. At $100/report from Cliros, his margin triples.
4. **AOL generation is tedious.** He knows the AOL format cold but still spends 45 minutes per letter pulling data from the search report into the template. A pre-filled draft would save him hours per week.
5. **Multi-county deals.** Occasionally gets properties near county lines or investors buying across 3-4 Georgia counties. Each county is a different abstractor relationship.

## What Would Make Him Pay

- **Accuracy above all.** If the first report misses a lien his abstractor would have caught, he's done. One mistake = malpractice exposure = never comes back.
- **Speed for competitive advantage.** If he can promise clients "title search done same-day," he wins deals from competitors.
- **AOL draft that's actually good.** Not a generic template — needs Fannie Mae B7-2-06 compliance, Georgia-specific language, and smart enough to flag when an AOL shouldn't be issued (complex title defects that need insurance).
- **PDF that looks professional.** He puts his firm's name on this. The report needs to look as good as what his abstractor produces — clean formatting, proper legal descriptions, citation format.

## What Would Make Him Leave

- False confidence — a report that says "clear title" when there's an unreleased mortgage
- Sloppy formatting or typos in legal descriptions
- No phone support when something goes wrong at the closing table
- If the platform sells data about his clients or closings to competitors

## Communication Style

Southern professional. Calls everyone "counselor" or by first name. Speaks in practical terms — "will this work at the closing table?" Not impressed by AI hype — wants to see it work on a property he already knows the answer for. Will test Cliros by running his last 5 closings through it and comparing to his abstractor's work. If all 5 match, he's in. If one is wrong, he needs to understand why before trusting it again.

## Biases

- Trusts people over technology — needs to feel like there's a human he can call
- Skeptical of Silicon Valley companies entering legal markets ("they don't understand liability")
- Believes AOLs will eventually replace title insurance for most residential transactions
- Protective of his client relationships — won't use any tool that inserts itself between him and his client
- Thinks the title insurance industry is "a cartel that's had it too good for too long"

## Key Question He Asks

"If I run my last closing through this and it misses the second mortgage that my abstractor caught — what's your answer? Because my bar license is on the line, not yours."
