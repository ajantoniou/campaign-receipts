## How She Sees Cliros

**Threat assessment: Medium.** Cliros threatens her abstractor business model but could also become a tool she uses herself.

### What worries her:
- If attorneys start running their own searches, they skip her agency entirely
- Her abstractors' specialized knowledge (knowing which county clerks index things wrong, which courts have legacy systems) is hard to replicate in AI
- Massachusetts has Torrens/registered land in some areas — extremely complex, AI will struggle
- She's seen "disruptors" come and go in title (Doma burned $124M and failed)

### What interests her:
- She could use Cliros as a first-pass tool and have her abstractors do verification — cuts their time in half
- Her abstractors cost $35-45/hour. If Cliros does 70% of the work, she needs fewer staff hours per file
- She struggles to hire young abstractors — nobody wants the job. AI could fill the gap.
- If Cliros offered a white-label version, she'd resell it to her attorney clients

### What would make her a customer:
- **White-label option** — she puts her brand on the report, charges her price
- **Bulk pricing** — 40-60 searches/month needs to be cheaper than $100 each
- **Integration with her workflow** — she uses RamQuest for her title production. API or export needed.
- **Quality flag system** — tell her WHAT the AI found and WHERE it's uncertain, so her abstractors know what to double-check

## Industry Knowledge She Brings

1. **Title plants are the real moat.** The Big Four (Fidelity, First American, Stewart, Old Republic) own private databases of county records going back 50+ years. No public API replicates this. Cliros will hit coverage gaps in counties where the Big Four have plant data but public records are incomplete.

2. **The 80/20 rule of title defects.** 80% of searches are clean — simple chain of title, no issues. AI will nail these. The other 20% have quirks: estates with missing heirs, old mechanics liens that were paid but never released, boundary disputes, tax sale redemption periods. These require human judgment.

3. **Massachusetts is special.** Registered land (Torrens), Land Court, the Abstract of Title system — MA has more edge cases than any other state. Don't launch in MA until you can handle registered land.

4. **Abstractor shortage is real.** Average abstractor age is 55+. Young people won't do courthouse research for $25-35/hour. In 10 years, there won't be enough abstractors. This is Cliros's tailwind — the industry NEEDS automation because the humans are retiring.

5. **Title insurance isn't going away.** AOLs are great for clean residential deals. But lenders on commercial transactions, high-value properties, and anything with defects will still require title insurance. Cliros should position as complementary, not a replacement.
