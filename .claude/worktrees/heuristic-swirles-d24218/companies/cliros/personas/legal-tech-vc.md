## How She Evaluates Cliros

### What excites her:

1. **Regulatory tailwind.** Fannie Mae AOL approval is a genuine structural shift. The last time the title industry had a regulatory catalyst this large was TRID in 2015. This is investable.

2. **Per-transaction model.** Revenue scales with real estate transaction volume, not seat count. In a hot market, revenue surges. Even in a down market, closings still happen (refis, foreclosures, estate sales).

3. **Empty competitive lane.** Self-serve, pay-per-use AI title search doesn't exist. The fact that National Attorney Title and ProTitle USA are service companies, not platforms, means the SaaS lane is genuinely open.

4. **Massive fragmentation.** 3,100+ counties with no standardized API = enormous data moat once you build county integrations. Every new county you add is a competitive barrier.

5. **Exit optionality.** At least 5 strategic acquirers (Qualia, Clio, First American, ServiceLink, ICE/Black Knight) would pay 8-12x revenue for this asset.

### What concerns her:

1. **Data sourcing risk.** If Pubrec/PropMix raises prices or cuts access, you're dead. Need multiple data sources and direct county integrations as backup.

2. **Accuracy liability.** One bad title search that leads to a closing defect = lawsuit. Even if the attorney is the one signing, they'll point at Cliros. Need E&O insurance and bulletproof disclaimers.

3. **Title insurance lobby.** ALTA (American Land Title Association) has spent decades protecting their industry. If AOLs threaten title insurance premiums meaningfully, expect regulatory pushback in state legislatures.

4. **Solo founder risk.** Building county data integrations, an AI analysis engine, and an attorney-facing product is a LOT for one person. The attorney cofounder (Tanner) needs to come in sooner rather than later — not for equity reasons but for credibility with customers.

5. **Market timing.** Real estate transaction volume is cyclical. If you launch during a slow market (2024-2025 has been sluggish), initial traction may be slow. This is fine for a bootstrapped company but could be a problem if you take venture money with growth expectations.

### What she'd want to see for a Series A ($5-8M at $40-60M pre):

- $1-2M ARR with 90%+ gross margins
- 200+ paying attorneys with <5% monthly churn
- 3+ states with strong county coverage
- Accuracy benchmarked: <2% error rate vs professional abstractor
- At least one insurance/lender partnership in pipeline
- Two-person founding team (technical + legal)
